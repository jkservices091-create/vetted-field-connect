# Roofing — Scenario Quiz

**Questions:** 10  |  **Passing score:** 8/10 (80%)  |  **Time limit:** 25 minutes

These are real-world judgment scenarios. Each question describes a job-site situation; pick the SINGLE BEST course of action. There is often more than one acceptable answer in real life — choose the one that best balances safety, code, and customer outcome.

---

1. **Scenario:** Your crew is mid-way through a full tear-off on a 2,400 sq ft, 8:12 pitch colonial in a northern climate for homeowner Mr. Burrows. Half the old shingles are stripped and you've reached a section over the garage where the decking is visibly soft — approximately 180 sq ft of OSB is punky, stained dark, and deforms underfoot. The original bid was for tear-off and re-shingle only. Replacing the rotted decking will add roughly $600 and a half-day to the job.

   **What do you do?**
   A. Shingle over the soft decking — asphalt shingles are flexible enough to bridge minor soft spots.
   B. Reinforce the soft area by screwing additional OSB overlay (1/4 inch) on top of the existing decking before shingling.
   C. Stop and contact Mr. Burrows immediately, show him the damaged decking with photos, provide a written change order for the repair, and replace all compromised decking before installing new roofing materials — do not shingle over unsound substrate.
   D. Replace the decking now without notifying Mr. Burrows and add the cost to the final invoice; he'll understand when he sees the receipt.

2. **Scenario:** Your crew is on a residential reroof and it is currently calm with light clouds. At 10 a.m., your phone weather radar shows a strong thunderstorm cell approximately 30 minutes away, moving toward the site. You have two workers on the 6:12 pitch roof. All old shingles are stripped, underlayment is partially installed, and no new shingles are down yet. The homeowners, the Castillo family, are at work.

   **What do you do?**
   A. Work faster — you can probably finish laying underlayment before the storm arrives.
   B. Get workers off the roof immediately. Nail down all loose underlayment and secure any exposed deck edges with whatever materials are on hand, then get everyone on the ground and away from the open roof before the storm arrives. Call the Castillos to inform them. Resume when conditions are safe.
   C. Keep one worker on the roof to monitor conditions and pull workers only when lightning is actually visible.
   D. Deploy a tarp over the exposed deck and let one worker secure the corners from the eave while another keeps watch from the ground.

3. **Scenario:** You are performing a re-shingle job on a 1990s two-story home. When you remove the ridge cap and top courses, you discover the existing attic ventilation consists of two small static box vents — no ridge vent, no soffit vents, and the box vents total only about 60 sq in of NFA for a 1,400 sq ft attic floor. The manufacturer of the new shingles explicitly states in the installation instructions that their warranty requires 1:150 net-free ventilation ratio unless a vapor barrier is present.

   **What do you do?**
   A. Install the new shingles per contract — ventilation is the homeowner's problem, not part of roofing.
   B. Document the deficient ventilation in writing, advise the homeowner that installing new shingles over a severely under-ventilated attic will likely void the manufacturer's warranty and accelerate shingle degradation, provide an estimate for corrective ventilation (continuous ridge vent + soffit vents), and obtain written acknowledgment before proceeding if the homeowner declines the correction.
   C. Install a ridge vent at no extra charge since it is required for a valid installation and you should have caught it in the bid.
   D. Install the new shingles and add a power attic ventilator — it is a faster fix than cutting new soffit vents.

4. **Scenario:** You are reroofing a 1,500 sq ft bungalow. As you install step flashing at the right sidewall where the roof meets the second-story exterior wall, you pull back the vinyl siding and find no kickout flashing at the bottom termination point. In the wall cavity behind the siding, you can see dark staining, soft wood framing, and what appears to be minor mold on the OSB sheathing — classic signs of years of water intrusion at exactly the location a missing kickout flashing would cause.

   **What do you do?**
   A. Install the kickout flashing now and complete the step flashing sequence — the wall damage is minimal and will dry out once the flashing is in place.
   B. Install the kickout flashing, document the wall damage with photos, and notify the homeowner in writing of the discovered rot and potential mold — recommend they have a contractor assess and remediate the wall framing and sheathing, since water intrusion damage must be properly evaluated before being concealed by new siding.
   C. Skip the kickout flashing since the homeowner did not ask about it in the original scope, and just caulk the bottom of the step flashing to redirect water.
   D. Stop the entire job and refuse to install flashing until the homeowner hires a remediation company to address the mold.

5. **Scenario:** You are starting a shingle installation on a residential home that has a 3-kilowatt solar array on the south-facing slope. The solar installer's company has removed the panels and stacked them on the roof deck nearby. While carrying bundles of shingles up the ladder, a crew member accidentally slides a bundle across a stacked solar panel, leaving a visible scratch across the tempered glass surface. The homeowner, Mr. Petersen, is not home. The solar company is not on-site.

   **What do you do?**
   A. Continue working and hope the scratch does not affect panel performance — tempered glass is durable.
   B. Stop using the solar array area as staging, photograph the damaged panel immediately, contact Mr. Petersen and your company's supervisor before continuing work, document the incident in writing, and report it honestly — your company's liability coverage will need to address the repair or replacement.
   C. Move the panels out of the way and proceed — if the scratch was already there, you cannot prove it wasn't.
   D. Ask the rest of your crew if they saw it happen; if no one confirms the incident, say nothing to the homeowner.

6. **Scenario:** You are reroofing a semi-detached home where the chimney sits on the property line and is structurally shared with the neighbor's home next door. The homeowner, Ms. Delacroix, wants new counter flashing installed. When you inspect the chimney, you find the mortar joints on the neighbor's side are severely eroded and the existing counter flashing on that side has separated completely from the masonry. Fixing only Ms. Delacroix's side will not create a watertight seal around the full chimney perimeter.

   **What do you do?**
   A. Flash and seal only Ms. Delacroix's side — the neighbor's deterioration is not your client's responsibility.
   B. Complete the counter flashing on Ms. Delacroix's side, document the neighbor-side deterioration with photos, advise Ms. Delacroix in writing that the chimney cannot be made fully weather-tight without cooperation from the neighboring unit, and recommend she contact her neighbor and possibly a shared-property attorney or HOA to coordinate a full chimney remediation.
   C. Flash both sides of the chimney and bill the full cost to Ms. Delacroix — she benefits from the complete seal.
   D. Refuse to install any flashing until both homeowners jointly authorize the work and share the cost equally.

7. **Scenario:** The homeowner, Mr. Whitfield, has a 15-year-old roof and is asking for a simple "roof-over" — a second layer of shingles applied over the existing layer — to save money. When you inspect the existing roof, you find the current shingle layer already has one roof-over beneath it. This would make the new installation a third layer. The local building code permits a maximum of two layers of asphalt shingles. Mr. Whitfield says "nobody will check" and that he just wants the cheapest fix.

   **What do you do?**
   A. Apply the third layer — it is the homeowner's property and he has accepted the risk.
   B. Explain to Mr. Whitfield that a third layer violates local building code and will be flagged if a permit is pulled or the home is sold. Recommend a full tear-off: the added weight of three layers exceeds typical structural design, and the existing layers likely have underlying issues that will compromise the new shingles. Provide separate quotes for tear-off/replace vs. roof-over (where the latter is not actually an option per code).
   C. Apply a single tab cap layer only over the ridge and problem areas without covering the whole roof, staying under the "new layer" threshold.
   D. Apply the third layer but advise Mr. Whitfield to disclose it to his insurer if he files a future claim.

8. **Scenario:** Your crew is on a 5:12 residential roof. You started the morning with winds calm at 5 mph. By noon, sustained winds have reached 22 mph with gusts to 30 mph. You are using personal fall arrest systems anchored to structural roof anchors. One worker says he's fine and wants to finish the valley they're in the middle of. The manufacturer of your PFAS equipment rates the harness system for use up to 25 mph sustained wind.

   **What do you do?**
   A. Continue work since sustained winds are still under the 25 mph equipment rating.
   B. Evaluate conditions beyond equipment ratings: at 22–30 mph, loose materials (shingle bundles, cut shingles, tools) become projectiles and workers' balance and footing on slope is compromised. Cease rooftop work, secure all loose materials, and resume when conditions are safe — PFAS ratings address fall arrest performance, not overall safe working conditions in wind.
   C. Allow the one worker to finish the valley since he is the most experienced and comfortable.
   D. Move all workers to the leeward (downwind) side of the roof and continue there since wind loading is lower.

9. **Scenario:** You are installing new shingles on a 1970s Cape Cod home for the Garcias. The original bid was for a standard 3-tab re-shingle. When you begin stripping the front slope, you discover the previous roofer installed new shingles directly over what appears to be the original 1970s wood shingles — a layer you were not told about. This adds a full day to the tear-off, additional disposal weight, and increases your material cost. The Garcias' total budget is tight and they have already paid a deposit.

   **What do you do?**
   A. Shingle over the wood shingle layer — it is solid and adds some insulation value.
   B. Stop tear-off, photograph the discovered layer, contact the Garcias to explain the situation, provide a written change order for the additional tear-off labor and disposal fees, and clearly explain that shingling over wood shingles is not permitted by most shingle manufacturers and creates a fire hazard and warranty void — proceed only after they authorize the change order.
   C. Complete the tear-off of all layers and absorb the extra cost to keep the customer relationship — include a note in the final invoice.
   D. Remove just the new shingles on top, leave the old wood shingles, and shingle over the wood layer since it was "original" and predates modern code restrictions.

10. **Scenario:** You are installing ice-and-water shield and underlayment on a new construction home in a cold climate (Zone 6). When you reach the eave at the front slope, you notice the contractor framed the eave overhang such that the exterior wall line falls 10 inches from the drip edge. Per the IRC, ice-and-water shield must extend to a point at least 24 inches inside the interior wall line. Your current roll of ice-and-water shield is 36 inches wide.

    **What do you do?**
    A. Run the 36-inch-wide ice-and-water shield from the drip edge — it extends roughly 26 inches up the slope, which likely reaches inside the wall line.
    B. Measure precisely: confirm the 24-inch interior wall line requirement is met by the membrane coverage, running a second overlapping course of ice-and-water shield up the slope if needed, before covering with standard underlayment. Do not assume the single roll width is sufficient without verifying the actual wall line location.
    C. Use standard felt underlayment from the drip edge since the overhang is only 10 inches and the ice-dam risk at the eave edge is low.
    D. Ask the GC if ice-and-water shield is really required — it is optional in Zone 6 if the attic is well ventilated.
