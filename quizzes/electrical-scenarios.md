# Electrical — Scenario Quiz

**Questions:** 10  |  **Passing score:** 8/10 (80%)  |  **Time limit:** 25 minutes

These are real-world judgment scenarios. Each question describes a job-site situation; pick the SINGLE BEST course of action. There is often more than one acceptable answer in real life — choose the one that best balances safety, code, and customer outcome.

---

1. **Scenario:** You're adding two new 20-amp circuits to a 1958 split-level home owned by Mr. and Mrs. Kowalski. When you open the main service panel, you find it is a 100-amp, 20-space panel with every slot filled. You also discover that two 15-amp breakers share a single terminal — one feeding the kitchen and one feeding the basement — both conductors torqued onto the same breaker lug. The panel is not a listed "tandem-breaker compatible" unit.

   **What do you do?**
   A. Install tandem (piggyback) breakers in the two slots where double-taps exist to free up space for the new circuits.
   B. Correct the double-taps by installing a sub-panel or appropriate tandem breakers only in listed positions, inform the Kowalskis in writing about the existing violation, and discuss upgrading service before proceeding with new circuits.
   C. Proceed with the new circuits by adding a small 4-space sub-panel fed from one of the existing double-tapped breakers, since that resolves the overcrowding.
   D. Advise the Kowalskis verbally that the panel is too small and leave without doing any work until they approve a full service upgrade.

2. **Scenario:** You are replacing a standard duplex receptacle in the bathroom of a 1972 ranch home. When you pull the old device, you find two-wire (no ground) NM cable feeding it. The homeowner, Mr. Tran, says he wants a three-prong grounded outlet because he uses a hair dryer and electric shaver there. You have GFCI receptacles on your truck.

   **What do you do?**
   A. Run a new grounded circuit from the panel to properly ground the bathroom receptacle before installing the three-prong outlet.
   B. Install a GFCI receptacle on the existing two-wire circuit and label it "No Equipment Ground," which is NEC-permitted and provides shock protection without a ground wire.
   C. Bond the ground screw of the new receptacle to the metal box using a short wire, since the metal box counts as a ground path in older homes.
   D. Install a standard three-prong receptacle and tell Mr. Tran the ground is "functionally adequate" because the house has a metal water pipe bond.

3. **Scenario:** You arrive at a 2001 colonial home to install a ceiling fan in the master bedroom. While removing the existing light fixture, you notice the branch circuit wiring in the box is aluminum — small-diameter branch-circuit aluminum wiring (not the large-feeder type). The existing device was a standard steel-screw terminal fixture, and you can see the conductors have the characteristic dull gray color and minor oxidation at the terminations.

   **What do you do?**
   A. Proceed with the ceiling fan installation using the existing wiring; aluminum branch wiring is safe as long as connections are tight.
   B. Pigtail the aluminum conductors to short copper leads using CO/ALR-rated wire nuts and connect the fan to the copper pigtails; note the aluminum wiring in writing to the homeowner and recommend a whole-home inspection.
   C. Use anti-oxidant compound on the existing steel-screw connections and proceed — anti-oxidant compound alone makes the installation safe.
   D. Stop work immediately, cap all wires, and refuse to continue until the entire branch circuit is rewired in copper.

4. **Scenario:** You are an electrician hired by a GC to run circuits on a remodel. A non-licensed handyman the GC also has on-site tells you he already wired three new bathroom receptacles yesterday, "saving time." He shows you the work: the receptacles are connected but the GFCI protection is missing, and you notice the neutral and ground wires have been swapped at one device — the neutral is on the green screw and the ground is on the silver screw. The GC is nearby and wants the job to move fast.

   **What do you do?**
   A. Ignore the handyman's work — it's not your circuit and not your liability. Continue your own scope.
   B. Fix only the reversed neutral/ground without saying anything; the GFCI issue is the GC's problem to sort out with the handyman.
   C. Immediately inform the GC that the handyman's wiring has a reversed neutral/ground (a shock and fire hazard) and missing GFCI protection (a code violation), and that you cannot energize those circuits or work alongside unlicensed electrical work without the violations being corrected. Document your notification in writing.
   D. Correct all of the handyman's work yourself, bill the GC for the extra time, and don't mention who did the original wiring.

5. **Scenario:** You are performing a service call at Mrs. Okonkwo's 1965 two-story home. She reports that her upstairs bathroom GFCI trips every time it rains heavily or after the lawn sprinklers run near the exterior wall. You test the circuit and find it is otherwise functional. When you open the GFCI device, you notice slight discoloration consistent with moisture intrusion. The exterior wall receptacle immediately below — on the same circuit — is in an old standard in-use cover that no longer seals properly.

   **What do you do?**
   A. Replace the GFCI device only; it has already worn out from repeated trips.
   B. Seal the exterior receptacle's in-use cover with caulk and reset the GFCI — the fault is cosmetic.
   C. Identify and correct the root cause: replace the degraded exterior cover with a properly rated in-use (while-in-use) weatherproof cover, inspect and seal any conduit or cable entry paths on the exterior wall, replace the moisture-damaged GFCI device, and confirm no wiring damage exists before re-energizing.
   D. Advise Mrs. Okonkwo to simply avoid using the bathroom receptacle during rainstorms and note the tripping as a nuisance issue.

6. **Scenario:** You are installing a new 200-amp service panel for a homeowner during a whole-home renovation. As you complete the work and are ready for the utility company connection, the homeowner, Mr. Delgado, mentions that his neighbor "who knows electricity" already bonded the neutral bar to the ground bar in the sub-panel feeding the detached garage — work done two years ago without a permit. Mr. Delgado asks if you can just leave it since "it's been fine."

   **What do you do?**
   A. Leave the sub-panel as-is; improper neutral-ground bonding in a sub-panel is a minor issue that rarely causes problems.
   B. Remove the neutral-ground bond in the sub-panel (the main bonding jumper belongs only in the main service panel), advise Mr. Delgado this must be corrected as it creates a shock hazard on equipment ground paths, and include it in your inspection documentation.
   C. Add an additional bonding jumper in the new main panel to compensate for the improper bond in the sub-panel.
   D. Notify the utility company of the sub-panel violation when they arrive to connect service.

7. **Scenario:** You are in the middle of replacing a 240-volt range receptacle in the kitchen of a 1990s home. You have turned off the breaker labeled "Range" and tested the receptacle with a non-contact voltage tester — it reads dead. A junior apprentice on your crew suggests skipping further verification and just starting the replacement since "you already tested it." You recall that the panel labels in this house are handwritten and several are known to be inaccurate.

   **What do you do?**
   A. Trust the non-contact tester result and proceed — a non-contact tester is sufficient verification.
   B. Have the homeowner verify the range does not turn on as a secondary confirmation.
   C. Before touching any conductors, verify zero energy with a calibrated digital multimeter (DMM) across all conductor combinations (L1-L2, L1-N, L2-N, L1-G, L2-G), then lock out and tag the breaker before proceeding.
   D. Turn off all breakers in the panel to eliminate any mislabeling risk, then proceed without a lock.

8. **Scenario:** You are doing a kitchen remodel and the permit requires AFCI protection on all new kitchen circuits per the current NEC. The homeowner, Ms. Reyes, says her neighbor's contractor "skipped the AFCI breakers on an identical job last month and saved $200." She asks you to do the same and offers to sign a waiver releasing you from liability. The AHJ is known to conduct final inspections on all permitted kitchen work.

   **What do you do?**
   A. Accept the waiver and install standard breakers — the homeowner's written release protects you legally.
   B. Explain to Ms. Reyes that a homeowner waiver cannot override adopted code requirements, that the inspector will flag missing AFCI protection at final inspection, and install AFCI breakers as required — offering to explain the cost difference and the fire protection benefit they provide.
   C. Install standard breakers, advise Ms. Reyes to request a different inspector, and keep the waiver on file.
   D. Install AFCI breakers but do not pull them through to the final inspection — tell the inspector the circuits are AFCI-protected without showing documentation.

9. **Scenario:** You discover active knob-and-tube (K&T) wiring in the walls and attic of a 1920s bungalow while roughing in new circuits for a bathroom addition. The existing K&T is energized and serving the living room lighting. The homeowner, Mr. Fitzpatrick, says his insurance company does not know about the K&T and asks you to "just leave it alone" and not document it. He is nervous about a rate increase.

   **What do you do?**
   A. Leave the K&T undisturbed and complete only your new-work scope — what you don't disturb is not your responsibility to report.
   B. Complete your new work, but document the presence of energized K&T wiring in your written job record, advise Mr. Fitzpatrick of the fire risk, and recommend a full K&T evaluation by a licensed electrician. Explain that you cannot conceal knowledge of a hazardous condition even if you are not the one correcting it.
   C. Cap and de-energize all discovered K&T immediately without a separate contract, since leaving it energized is a life-safety issue.
   D. Agree to say nothing, but send Mr. Fitzpatrick a certified letter after the job suggesting he get it inspected.

10. **Scenario:** You are finishing a basement for a homeowner and have been running new 15-amp lighting circuits. Near the end of the job, you realize the circuit you've been installing is actually on a 20-amp breaker you put in — you used 14 AWG wire throughout the basement run, which is protected by a 20-amp breaker. The basement is already drywalled and the homeowner is eager to schedule the final inspection tomorrow.

    **What do you do?**
    A. Leave it — 14 AWG on a 20-amp breaker is only a minor overcurrent risk and inspectors rarely catch it.
    B. Replace the 20-amp breaker with a 15-amp breaker. 14 AWG is rated at 15 amps; the fix is at the panel without opening the walls.
    C. Add a second run of 12 AWG in parallel with the 14 AWG to bring the combined capacity up to 20 amps.
    D. Notify the homeowner that the inspection must be postponed, open the wall, and replace all 14 AWG with 12 AWG before proceeding.
