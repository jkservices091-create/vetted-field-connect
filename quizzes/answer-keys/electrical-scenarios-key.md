# Electrical — Scenario Quiz Answer Key

1. **B** — A fully occupied panel with double-tapped breakers is a code violation (NEC 408.21 requires each conductor to have its own terminal unless the breaker is listed for multiple conductors). Installing tandem breakers in an unlisted panel (Answer A) creates another violation. The correct call is to notify the homeowner in writing, correct the double-taps, and evaluate whether the service capacity is adequate before adding new loads. Leaving without doing any work (Answer D) is unnecessarily disruptive when a proper correction path exists.

2. **B** — NEC 406.4(D)(2) expressly permits replacing an ungrounded two-wire receptacle with a GFCI type, provided it is labeled "No Equipment Ground." This provides shock protection equivalent to a grounded circuit for the user without the cost of running a new circuit. Answer A (running a new circuit) is technically ideal but not required by code and disproportionately expensive for a single receptacle. Answer C (bonding to a metal box) is not a valid grounding path under the NEC for this purpose. Answer D (claiming "functionally adequate" ground) is incorrect and dangerous — a water pipe bond does not provide an equipment ground to a receptacle.

3. **B** — Small-diameter aluminum branch circuit wiring (introduced in the 1960s–1970s) is a recognized fire hazard when terminated on devices not rated for aluminum (CO/ALR devices required per NEC 406.9). Pigtailing with CO/ALR-rated connectors and copper leads is the accepted remediation method. Simply proceeding (Answer A) ignores a known hazard. Anti-oxidant compound alone (Answer C) does not address the incompatible device connection. Refusing all work (Answer D) is unnecessarily severe — the CPSC and NEC provide approved repair methods.

4. **C** — A reversed neutral/ground (Answer B partial fix aside) creates a shock hazard: energized metal frames, incorrect GFCI operation, and potential fire risk at the device. Missing GFCI in a bathroom violates NEC 210.8. Ignoring the work entirely (Answer A) exposes workers and occupants to hazards you have observed and have a duty to report. The GC and the licensed electrician of record have an obligation not to energize circuits with life-safety violations. Written documentation protects you professionally; verbal notice alone (Answer D) may not be sufficient if an incident occurs.

5. **C** — Recurring GFCI trips correlated with moisture events point to a ground fault caused by water entry, most commonly through a compromised exterior enclosure on the downstream receptacle. Simply replacing the GFCI device (Answer A) treats the symptom. Caulking (Answer B) is a cosmetic repair that does not address wiring damage or enclosure rating. A proper in-use (while-in-use) weatherproof cover (NEMA 3R minimum per NEC 406.9) combined with a sealed cable/conduit entry is the root-cause fix. The damaged GFCI device itself should be replaced since moisture intrusion may have compromised its internal components and trip threshold.

6. **B** — The main bonding jumper (neutral-to-ground bond) must exist only in the main service panel, not in sub-panels (NEC 250.24 and 250.142). A neutral-ground bond in a sub-panel puts the grounding conductor in parallel with the neutral return path, energizing equipment grounds and metal enclosures at the detached structure — a serious shock hazard to anyone touching grounded equipment. Leaving it (Answer A) creates ongoing hazard. Adding another bond at the main panel (Answer C) does not fix the sub-panel problem and compounds the parallel path issue. Notifying the utility company (Answer D) is not their jurisdiction; this is a wiring defect to be corrected by the electrician.

7. **C** — Non-contact voltage testers (NCVs) can give false-negative readings due to shielded cable, low voltage induction, or battery/sensor issues. On a panel with known mislabeled breakers, a single NCV test is not adequate verification of zero-energy. OSHA 1926 Subpart K and NFPA 70E both require voltage verification with a rated meter across all conductor pairs before touching conductors. Locking and tagging the breaker (lock out/tag out) is required. Having the homeowner test the appliance (Answer B) does not verify conductor voltage. Shutting down all breakers (Answer D) may affect occupied spaces, is unnecessarily disruptive, and still does not constitute proper LOTO.

8. **B** — A homeowner waiver cannot override adopted codes under any jurisdiction — this is settled law. NEC AFCI requirements for kitchen circuits exist in current code editions, and the AHJ inspector will cite missing AFCI protection at final inspection, causing a failed inspection and delay. The contractor (not the homeowner) bears code compliance responsibility. Advising the homeowner to request a different inspector (Answer C) is ethically improper. Installing AFCI breakers and misrepresenting the installation to an inspector (Answer D) is fraud.

9. **B** — Knob-and-tube wiring carries real fire risks (CPSC, NFPA data), especially where it has been altered, is in contact with insulation, or shows deterioration. You cannot agree to conceal material safety information as a licensed professional — doing so exposes you to liability if a fire occurs. You are not required to correct work outside your contracted scope, but you are required to document and advise on known hazardous conditions. Capping energized K&T without a separate contract and without verifying the circuit can be safely de-energized (Answer C) creates new hazards. Sending a letter after the fact (Answer D) after agreeing to silence is not adequate professional conduct.

10. **B** — The fix is at the overcurrent device. NEC 240.4 requires conductors to be protected at their ampacity: 14 AWG copper is rated 15 amps, not 20 amps. Replacing the 20-amp breaker with a 15-amp breaker corrects the overcurrent protection without opening any walls, since the breaker is the issue, not the wiring method. Leaving the 14 AWG on a 20-amp breaker (Answer A) is a code violation that risks overheated wiring inside a finished wall. Running conductors in parallel (Answer C) is not permitted for conductors under 1/0 AWG per NEC 310.10(H). A full wall re-open (Answer D) is unnecessary since the violation is correctable at the panel.
