# HVAC — Answer Key

1. **B** — EPA Section 608 requires anyone who purchases refrigerants in containers larger than 2 lbs (for high-GWP refrigerants) or handles refrigerants in HVAC/R work to hold an EPA 608 certification. Universal certification covers all system types.

2. **B** — Intentional venting of refrigerants covered under Section 608 is illegal. Refrigerant must be recovered using an EPA-certified recovery machine and stored in DOT-approved, labeled recovery cylinders before opening any part of the refrigerant circuit.

3. **B** — Manual J is ACCA's residential load calculation procedure used to determine the correct heating and cooling capacity the equipment must deliver for a specific home. Oversized or undersized equipment causes comfort, efficiency, and humidity-control problems.

4. **B** — Brazing produces intense UV light, hot metal surfaces, and flux fumes containing zinc and fluoride compounds. Safety glasses, fire-resistant gloves, and ventilation are the baseline requirements; a respirator is added when working in confined spaces.

5. **B** — Condensate drain lines require a minimum 1/4-inch-per-foot slope to allow gravity drainage. Flatter lines allow condensate to pool, promoting mold growth and potential drain pan overflow.

6. **B** — Negative-pressure air handlers draw air in through any opening. Without a trap, the negative pressure overcomes the gravity drain and pulls air back into the unit, breaking the condensate flow. The trap maintains a water seal that allows condensate out while blocking air entry.

7. **B** — The suction line carries cold, low-pressure refrigerant vapor returning to the compressor. Without insulation, warm ambient air condenses on the line, causing moisture damage and heat gain that reduces system efficiency and can lead to compressor flooding.

8. **C** — Category IV appliances (high-efficiency condensing furnaces with flue gas temperatures below 140°F) produce acidic condensate and operate under positive vent pressure. They require a sealed, positive-pressure, corrosion-resistant vent material such as Schedule 40 PVC or CPVC rated for this service.

9. **B** — The IMC and NFPA 54 require two permanent openings for combustion air in a confined space — one within 12 inches of the ceiling and one within 12 inches of the floor — to provide both combustion air supply and relief for flue gas spillage prevention.

10. **B** — High-MERV filters have significantly higher airflow resistance than standard filters. If the blower cannot overcome the added static pressure, airflow across the evaporator coil drops below design, causing coil freeze-up in cooling mode and potential heat exchanger overheating in heating mode.

11. **B** — The blower's published performance data includes a maximum rated external static pressure (ESP). Measuring TESP and comparing it to the blower's rated ESP tells the technician whether the duct system is restricting airflow beyond equipment limits.

12. **C** — Return air must never be drawn from a garage or from spaces containing combustion appliances unless they are properly isolated, because doing so can distribute carbon monoxide, vehicle exhaust, or combustion gases throughout the living space.

13. **C** — In standard 24-volt thermostat wiring, blue (or sometimes black) is used for the common (C) wire, which completes the 24V circuit from the transformer. Red is power (R), white is heat (W), yellow is cooling (Y), and green is the fan (G).

14. **B** — Superheat charging is used with fixed-orifice (piston) metering devices because the orifice size doesn't modulate. Measuring suction line superheat ensures the evaporator is fully active without liquid refrigerant reaching the compressor. TXV systems use subcooling as the primary charging method.

15. **B** — Pressurizing with dry nitrogen (an inert, non-flammable gas) to the system's rated test pressure and checking for leaks with soap solution or an electronic detector is the correct procedure before introducing refrigerant. Compressed air introduces moisture and oxygen; refrigerant leak-testing is done only after a successful nitrogen test.

16. **A** — When changing refrigerant types (e.g., R-22 to R-410A), the existing line set must be flushed of residual oil and the indoor coil replaced with one rated and pressure-tested for the new refrigerant. Mixing refrigerants or using incompatible components is a safety and performance violation.

17. **B** — A yellow, wavering flame in a gas furnace indicates incomplete combustion, most often from insufficient primary air, a dirty or misaligned burner, or a blocked heat exchanger port. A cracked heat exchanger can cause disturbance in flame pattern but is a separate safety concern that should also be evaluated.

18. **B** — Energy codes (IECC) require supply and return ducts in unconditioned spaces to be insulated to R-6 minimum in most climate zones, with R-8 required in hotter or colder zones. Uninsulated sheet metal ducts in attics lose a significant fraction of conditioned air energy.

19. **C** — The suction line service valve is on the low-pressure side of the system, between the evaporator outlet and the compressor inlet. This is where a manifold gauge set low-side hose is connected to measure suction pressure and calculate superheat.

20. **B** — Heavy frost on the outdoor coil in heating mode that does not clear indicates the defrost cycle is not initiating or completing. The defrost control board (which monitors time and temperature) and the defrost thermostat (which senses coil temperature) are the primary components to check before assuming a refrigerant or sizing issue.
