# Drywall & Plaster — Answer Key

1. **C** — ½-inch gypsum board is the standard panel for walls on 16-inch stud spacing in residential construction. ⅝-inch Type X is reserved for fire-rated assemblies; ¼-inch is used for curved surfaces or lamination only.

2. **B** — Industry standards (GA-216) require field screws at no more than 12 inches on center for walls and 12 inches for ceilings. Edge fasteners follow a tighter schedule, but field screws at 16 or 24 inches are insufficient to prevent panel sag or fastener pops.

3. **A** — A properly driven screw creates a slight dimple in the face paper without tearing it. Breaking through the paper destroys the holding power of that fastener; a proud screw will telegraph through compound and must be re-driven.

4. **B** — ⅝-inch Type X gypsum board contains glass fibers and other additives that retard burn-through. A single layer on each side of a wood-stud wall is the most common assembly for a 1-hour fire-resistance rating per UL design criteria.

5. **B** — Control joints are relief cuts that allow the drywall field to move independently of the framing. Without them, seasonal movement and structural deflection concentrate stress at random locations, causing visible cracking in large ceiling or wall expanses.

6. **B** — Drywall joint compound contains crystalline silica as a filler. Repeated inhalation of respirable silica dust causes silicosis, a serious and irreversible lung disease. OSHA's silica standard (29 CFR 1926.1153) applies to drywall sanding operations.

7. **B** — A Level 5 finish requires a full skim coat of finish or topping compound over the entire board surface, not just the joints and fasteners. This eliminates surface texture variations that show through high-sheen paint or in raking light — the defining step beyond Level 4.

8. **C** — Hanging panels perpendicular to ceiling joists means the long, factory-tapered edges run across multiple framing members, reducing the total number of butt joints (square-cut ends) and distributing any deflection load more evenly across the panel.

9. **B** — Expanded metal corner bead is the most impact-resistant option because the metal is rigid and anchors deeply into compound. Paper-faced vinyl and bullnose paper beads offer better crack resistance but less impact protection in high-traffic areas.

10. **B** — MR board ("greenboard") is rated for areas of incidental moisture exposure such as behind sinks or vanities. It is not approved by the IRC or most tile industry standards (TCNA) as a direct substrate inside a shower surround or as a pan liner.

11. **A** — Taping (or all-purpose) compound has higher shrinkage and dries harder, making it suitable for embedding tape and first coats. Topping compound has a finer texture, less shrinkage, and sands more easily, making it ideal for finish coats. Using taping compound as a finish coat produces a hard, gritty surface that is difficult to sand smooth.

12. **B** — Ridges and tool marks left on a dried coat must be knocked down with a broad knife and lightly sanded flat before the next coat is applied. Burying them with more compound builds a crown that photographs through paint; re-wetting and troweling a dry coat is ineffective and risks delamination.

13. **B** — Fine-thread (also called S-type) drywall screws are designed to self-drill through light-gauge metal studs without stripping. Coarse-thread screws are for wood framing; nails and staples are inappropriate for metal-framed assemblies.

14. **B** — Fire-rated assembly designs (UL listings) require that butt joints on opposite wall faces be staggered to different studs. Back-to-back butt joints create a thermal weak point that can compromise the hourly rating of the assembly.

15. **C** — Drywall should be held ½ inch off a concrete slab to prevent wicking moisture from the slab into the panel edge, which can cause delamination, mold growth, and paper deterioration. A ½-inch gap is the standard minimum clearance.

16. **B** — Brown coat plaster must be applied over a scratch coat that has been moist-cured and is firm (green), not fully dry and hard. A fully dry scratch coat absorbs water too quickly from the brown coat, causing shrinkage cracks; a wet scratch coat will not provide adequate suction or mechanical bond.

17. **C** — Joint photographing (also called shadowing or banding) in raking light is caused by a crowned ridge at the edge of the compound coat — the result of insufficient coats, inadequate feathering width, or both. Wider, thinner feathering on each successive coat eliminates the crown.

18. **B** — GA-216 and most manufacturer guidelines call for control joints at maximum 30-foot intervals in ceilings with no perimeter relief. Where perimeter relief exists (floating angles), the spacing can extend to 50 feet in one direction.

19. **B** — Airborne texture mist contains fine particles that can irritate or damage the respiratory tract. Safety glasses protect eyes from overspray. An N95 or better particulate respirator is the minimum respiratory protection; a supplied-air unit is not required for water-based texture under normal conditions.

20. **C** — The IRC requires that the garage ceiling below a habitable room be covered with ⅝-inch Type X gypsum board (or equivalent) to provide a minimum fire separation between the garage and living space above. Standard ½-inch drywall does not meet this fire-resistance requirement.
