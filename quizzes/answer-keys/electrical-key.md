# Electrical — Answer Key

1. **B** — The proper LOTO sequence is: identify all energy sources, isolate them, apply a personal lock, attach a tag, and verify zero energy with a calibrated meter before touching any conductors. Skipping any step creates an electrocution hazard.

2. **C** — Per NEC Table 310.12, 12 AWG copper has an ampacity of 20 amperes at 60°C. 14 AWG is rated for only 15 amperes; using it on a 20-ampere circuit is a code violation.

3. **C** — The NEC does not require GFCI protection for interior receptacles in rooms with no water sources, such as a dedicated home office. Bathrooms, garages, and outdoor locations are all explicitly listed as GFCI-required spaces.

4. **B** — NEC Section 210.11(C)(1) requires at least two 20-ampere small-appliance branch circuits for countertop receptacles in a kitchen. This ensures capacity for multiple simultaneous appliances.

5. **B** — NEC Section 406.12 requires tamper-resistant receptacles for all 15- and 20-ampere, 125-volt receptacles in dwelling units — not just in kitchens or bedrooms. The requirement applies throughout the home.

6. **C** — Using the NEC box fill rules (2.25 cu in per 12 AWG conductor, 2.25 for the device yoke counted as two conductors, and the largest ground counted once), the required fill exceeds 18 cubic inches. An 18 cu in or larger box is needed.

7. **B** — The NEC requires AFCI protection for all 120-volt, 15- and 20-ampere circuits in bedrooms of dwelling units. The requirement applies regardless of circuit length, wiring material, or whether the circuit serves receptacles or lighting.

8. **C** — NEC 110.26(A)(1) requires a minimum of 30 inches of clear working space in front of electrical panels up to 150V to ground in a dwelling. This allows a worker to stand in front safely and opens equipment doors fully.

9. **D** — The NEC permits enclosed, surface-mounted LED or fluorescent luminaires in clothes closets with no minimum clearance from storage, provided they are listed for the location. Pendant-hung and open-lamp fixtures have clearance restrictions.

10. **A** — Metal water piping that may become energized must be bonded to the grounded service equipment — specifically the grounded (neutral) bus or equipment grounding bus in the main panel — to ensure a fault will clear the overcurrent device.

11. **B** — Bathroom exhaust fans can accumulate moisture inside the housing or duct. If water reaches the fan motor windings or wiring, it creates a ground fault that trips the GFCI. The fan wiring and housing should be inspected for moisture intrusion.

12. **B** — NEC 334.15 permits NM-B cable in unfinished basements of single-family dwellings only when installed in conduit or secured along structural members with physical damage protection. Exposed horizontal runs across open framing require conduit or protection.

13. **B** — NEC 210.12 and NFPA 72 require residential smoke alarms to be AC-powered with battery backup and interconnected so that activating one alarm triggers all alarms throughout the dwelling, maximizing evacuation time.

14. **B** — A repeatedly tripping breaker is doing its job protecting the circuit. The correct response is to investigate the cause — overload, short circuit, or ground fault — before resetting. Upsizing the breaker without investigation is a serious safety violation.

15. **C** — A 30-ampere, 240-volt dryer circuit requires 10 AWG copper conductors per NEC Table 310.12. Using 12 AWG would create an overcurrent hazard since the wire's ampacity would be less than the breaker rating.

16. **B** — Carbon monoxide alarms are required outside each separate sleeping area and on every habitable level. CO is nearly the same density as air, so ceiling, wall, or plug-in placement all provide adequate detection; the "CO sinks" or "CO rises" myths are incorrect.

17. **B** — Pigtailing is the preferred method when a device is in the middle of a circuit run. It ensures that if the device is removed or fails, the downstream outlets continue to function, and the device is not relied upon as a splice point.

18. **B** — The grounded (neutral) conductor carries normal return current back to the source. The equipment grounding (bare or green) conductor carries current only during a fault, providing a low-impedance path that trips the breaker and eliminates shock hazard.

19. **B** — Exterior receptacles require at minimum a NEMA 3R (rainproof) enclosure. When the receptacle may be in use with a cord plugged in, an in-use (bubble) cover that maintains weatherproof integrity with the cord in place is also required.

20. **C** — Installing the GFCI device at the first outlet in the run and connecting all downstream receptacles to the LOAD terminals provides GFCI protection to the entire circuit with a single device, which is the most cost-effective and code-compliant approach.
