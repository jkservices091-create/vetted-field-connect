# Insulation & Air Sealing — Answer Key

1. **B** — The batt must fill the entire cavity depth without gaps behind it. Using the correct-depth batt or adding a supplemental strip prevents the convective air loop that forms behind under-depth batts and degrades thermal performance.

2. **C** — OSHA and most manufacturers require at minimum an N95 particulate respirator when working with airborne fiberglass or mineral wool fibers. A surgical mask does not provide adequate filtration; a supplied-air unit is not required for this task.

3. **B** — R-value (thermal resistance) quantifies a material's ability to resist heat flow; the higher the R-value, the better the thermal performance. It does not measure vapor permeance, compressive strength, or air permeance.

4. **C** — An air barrier resists bulk airflow (convection) through the envelope. Vapor barriers or retarders address vapor diffusion. Confusing the two is a common field mistake that leads to incorrect material selection.

5. **B** — IC-rated (Insulation-Contact rated) fixtures are listed for direct contact with insulation. Non-IC fixtures must be kept at least 3 inches away from insulation and require an air-gap dam to prevent fire hazard.

6. **B** — Two-part closed-cell spray foam adheres to irregular surfaces, expands to fill voids, and provides a durable air seal at top plates. Stuffed fiberglass batts are not an effective air barrier; tape alone may not bridge rough framing gaps reliably.

7. **B** — Attic baffles maintain the required ventilation channel between the insulation surface and the roof sheathing, allowing outside air to move from soffit vents to the ridge. Without them, insulation blocks the soffits and defeats the ventilation system.

8. **C** — Closed-cell SPF typically achieves R-6 to R-7 per inch (vs. R-3.5–4 for open-cell) and has low vapor permeance (≤1 perm at certain thicknesses), classifying it as a Class II vapor retarder in many code jurisdictions.

9. **B** — A blower door depressurizes or pressurizes the building to measure total air leakage (ACH50 or CFM50). It does not measure R-value, duct static pressure, or free-area ventilation ratios.

10. **C** — OSHA's PPE standard (29 CFR 1910.132) requires eye/face protection whenever there is reasonable probability of injury. SPF chemicals (isocyanates, polyols) are eye irritants and sensitizers; eye protection is always required during application.

11. **B** — Spray polyurethane foam is a plastic and will ignite and spread flame. Building codes require an approved thermal or ignition barrier (typically ½-inch drywall or an equivalent listed coating) between the foam and any occupied space.

12. **D** — Variable-perm ("smart") membranes adjust their permeance based on ambient humidity, allowing drying in summer while restricting vapor drive in winter — ideal for mixed and cold climates. Foil (Class I) can trap moisture; kraft (Class II) is fixed-perm; latex paint (Class III) alone is rarely sufficient.

13. **C** — Compressing a batt reduces its loft and effective R-value. Manufacturers rate R-values at the labeled thickness; installing a batt thicker than the cavity depth compresses it and lowers thermal performance proportionally.

14. **B** — Dense-pack cellulose is installed at 3.5–4 lb/ft³ to prevent settling and provide some air resistance within the cavity. Lower densities settle significantly; higher densities exceed equipment capacity and can stress wall sheathing.

15. **C** — IRC Table N1102.1.2 (2021 edition) requires R-38 attic insulation in Climate Zones 4C and lower Zone 5 areas, with Zone 5 and above requiring R-49 in many configurations. Zone 1 requires only R-30. (Note: exact values vary by edition; the principle is that colder zones demand higher R.)

16. **A** — Both A and B components must be within the temperature range specified by the manufacturer (commonly 60–90°F) for the correct mix ratio and cure. Off-ratio foam caused by cold components will be tacky, weak, or off-ratio — a frequent field defect.

17. **B** — A foam gasket behind the cover plate stops airflow at the plate/drywall interface, and sealing the wiring holes into the stud cavity addresses the source. Caulking only the visible perimeter or stuffing batt behind the box leaves the main air path open.

18. **B** — Mineral wool is manufactured from rock or slag and is inherently non-combustible (melting point >1,800°F). It can be used in fire-resistance-rated assemblies as a fire block or contribute to hourly ratings — a significant advantage over fiberglass batts.

19. **B** — Loose-fill insulation compacts under its own weight over time. Installers must install to a greater initial depth so that once settling occurs, the remaining depth still delivers the specified R-value. Attic rulers account for both installed and settled depths.

20. **A** — Sealing a crawlspace can trap radon that enters through the soil. A radon assessment or mitigation strategy must be addressed before encapsulation. Inadvertently sealing radon in can elevate indoor concentrations to unhealthy levels.
