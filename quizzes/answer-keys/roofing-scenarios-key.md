# Roofing — Scenario Quiz Answer Key

1. **C** — Shingling over compromised decking (Answers A and B) violates virtually every manufacturer's installation specification, which requires sound substrate for warranty validity, and creates a structural and water-management failure point under the new roof. An OSB overlay (Answer B) does not fix punky substrate — it bridges the soft area without providing adequate nail pullout resistance. Proceeding without owner authorization and billing after the fact (Answer D) removes the homeowner's right to make an informed decision about their property and budget. A change order is the correct mechanism for discovered scope additions on a fixed-bid job; most homeowners will authorize legitimate deck repair when shown the evidence.

2. **B** — Lightning and approaching storms are non-negotiable stop-work triggers for rooftop work under OSHA 1926 Subpart Q safety guidelines and general industry practice. Once workers are on the ground, securing loose underlayment prevents it from becoming a tarp-sized projectile or from peeling back during the storm, which would leave the exposed deck unprotected. Continuing to work faster (Answer A) risks a lightning strike, wind-driven fall, or flash storm arrival faster than forecast. Keeping a monitor on the roof (Answer C) and working on tarps from the eave during wind (Answer D) both leave workers in harm's way during an escalating hazard.

3. **B** — Manufacturer warranty requirements are a contractual obligation that travels with the product. Installing a warranted product in a configuration that voids that warranty exposes both the homeowner (who loses the warranty) and the roofer (who installs a knowingly deficient system) to future liability. Power attic ventilators (Answer D) create negative pressure and can actually pull conditioned air out of the living space and introduce combustion back-drafting risks — they are not equivalent to balanced passive ventilation. The correct approach is to price the ventilation correction, inform the homeowner of the warranty implications, and get written acknowledgment before proceeding on a substandard installation if they decline.

4. **B** — Installing the kickout flashing is the correct and necessary roofing action (Answer A is partially right), but stopping there and closing the wall without disclosing discovered rot and mold would conceal a structural and health hazard you have personally observed. Homeowners have a right to know about significant damage inside their own walls. Kickout flashing prevents future water entry; it does not reverse existing wood rot or remediate existing mold. Skipping the flashing and using caulk (Answer C) is an inadequate substitute — caulk fails and does not create the physical diversion geometry of a kickout. Shutting down the entire job (Answer D) over a contractor referral is disproportionate.

5. **B** — Honesty when damage occurs on a job site is both a professional and legal obligation. Attempting to conceal the incident (Answers C and D) is a form of fraud that could result in contract termination, lawsuits, and loss of licensing. Hoping the scratch does not affect performance (Answer A) ignores that even cosmetic damage to a solar panel's tempered glass can create micro-stress fractures that grow over thermal cycling. Stopping work in the solar panel zone and properly staging materials away from the panels protects everyone going forward. Documenting and reporting promptly demonstrates professionalism and allows your company's insurance to handle the claim correctly.

6. **B** — A chimney shared between two properties is a shared structure with shared maintenance obligations. Flashing only one side (Answer A) creates a half-sealed chimney that will continue to leak from the neighbor's side — money wasted for the homeowner. Flashing both sides and billing only Ms. Delacroix (Answer C) creates a situation where you performed work on the neighbor's property without authorization — a liability issue. Refusing to work until costs are split (Answer D) is unnecessarily adversarial and not your role to enforce. The correct path is to do your contracted scope, document the adjacent deficiencies, and give the homeowner the information she needs to resolve the shared-structure issue through appropriate channels.

7. **B** — Local building codes typically limit residential asphalt shingle installations to two layers maximum (IRC R905.2.6 and most local amendments). A third layer exceeds structural design load assumptions for residential roof framing. The homeowner's statement that "nobody will check" does not change your legal obligation — applying a third layer is a code violation you would be performing and signing off on. Partial cap layer application (Answer C) is not a recognized method and would still be a violation. Advising disclosure to an insurer after the fact (Answer D) does not correct the code violation and sets you up for liability if a roof failure causes property damage.

8. **B** — Equipment ratings do not define the full envelope of safe working conditions. At 22–30 mph, loose roofing materials become projectiles that can injure workers or people below, footing on a sloped surface is compromised, and ladder stability is reduced. OSHA 1926.502 fall protection requirements do not override the requirement to stop work when conditions create hazards beyond what PPE controls. The PFAS rating addresses arrest force in a fall, not environmental conditions that make falling more likely. Working only on the leeward side (Answer D) still leaves workers on a slope with wind and projectile hazards and is not a recognized OSHA-compliant mitigation.

9. **B** — Wood shingles as a substrate for asphalt shingles are prohibited by virtually all asphalt shingle manufacturers and are a fire hazard (wood shingles are Class C or unrated; placing new asphalt over them prevents proper substrate inspection and creates a combustible void space). Shingling over wood (Answers A and D) voids the manufacturer warranty and creates a code violation in most jurisdictions. A change order is the correct and transparent mechanism to address discovered scope changes — absorbing the cost silently (Answer C) is financially unsustainable and sets a bad precedent. The Garcias deserve to understand what was found and why it must be addressed.

10. **B** — The IRC requires ice-and-water shield to extend at least 24 inches measured from the interior face of the exterior wall, not from the drip edge. With a 10-inch overhang, the interior wall line is roughly 10 inches from the drip edge — meaning a 36-inch-wide membrane barely covers the required zone, and any variation in application (waves, short laps) may leave a gap. Measuring precisely and installing a second course if needed costs minimal additional material and ensures IRC R905.2.7 compliance. Relying on the assumption that one course is sufficient (Answer A) without measuring is the type of error that causes ice-dam claims. Ice-and-water shield in Zone 6 is mandatory, not optional (Answer D).
