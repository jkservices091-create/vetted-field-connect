# Siding & Exterior Finish — Answer Key

1. **B** — Housewrap must be lapped like shingles: upper over lower by a minimum of 6 inches (most manufacturer instructions specify 6–12 inches). Reverse lapping allows water to infiltrate behind the WRB and reach the sheathing.

2. **B** — A kickout (diverter) flashing at the base of a wall-roof intersection diverts water that would otherwise run along the step flashing and straight down behind the siding. Without it, water is directed behind the cladding and into the wall framing.

3. **C** — Fiber cement manufacturer installation guides (James Hardie, Nichiha, etc.) and the IRC require a minimum 6-inch clearance between the bottom edge of fiber cement and finished grade to prevent prolonged moisture contact and wicking, which causes swelling and paint failure.

4. **A** — Fiber cement must be fastened with stainless-steel or hot-dipped galvanized (HDG) nails of at least 6d (2-inch) length. Electrogalvanized (bright EG) coatings deteriorate quickly in contact with fiber cement's alkaline composition, causing corrosion and staining.

5. **B** — Vinyl siding has a high coefficient of thermal expansion. Nails must be driven with approximately 1/32-inch (roughly the thickness of a playing card) clearance between the nail head and the panel face so the siding can move laterally without buckling or cracking.

6. **B** — Vinyl siding end joints must be staggered course to course (not lined up vertically) to prevent a continuous path for wind-driven water. Joints should also be oriented so they face away from the most visible sight lines and high-traffic views.

7. **B** — Z-flashing (drip edge) above windows must be installed over the housewrap. This puts it on the exterior of the drainage plane so any water running down the wall sheds over the top of the window instead of behind the WRB.

8. **B** — At vertical seams the drainage plane must be maintained: the upslope or inward piece laps over the downslope or outward piece. Taping alone without proper lapping is insufficient because tape can fail and water will follow the un-lapped seam inward.

9. **B** — Most engineered wood and hardboard siding manufacturers require a minimum 2-inch clearance above horizontal surfaces (roofs, decks, concrete). This prevents the bottom edge from sitting in standing water, which causes delamination and rot.

10. **B** — Three-coat stucco is applied scratch coat first (keyed into the lath), then the brown coat (float to plane), then the finish coat (texture and color). Reversing the sequence does not allow proper mechanical keying or curing between coats.

11. **A** — Industry standards (ASTM C1063) require metal lath to be lapped a minimum of 1/2 inch at side (vertical) laps and 1 inch at end (horizontal) laps. Insufficient lap can result in lath separation and stucco cracking.

12. **B** — A rainscreen gap (typically 3/8 to 3/4 inch) between the back of the cladding and the WRB allows capillary-driven moisture that penetrates the cladding to drain out at the base and allows drying to occur, preventing rot and mold in the sheathing.

13. **C** — Correct face-nailing for lap siding drives the nail through the face of the siding into the stud above the top edge of the lower course. This "blind nails" the lower board while allowing the upper course to cover the nail head, preventing water infiltration at the nail hole.

14. **B** — Caulk belongs at transitions between siding and trim (window and door perimeters, corner boards, horizontal trim intersections). Caulking horizontal lap joints between field courses traps moisture and prevents drainage; those joints are designed to shed water without caulk.

15. **B** — OSHA 29 CFR 1926.501(b)(13) requires fall protection at 6 feet or more in residential construction, including workers on ladders or scaffolding installing siding.

16. **C** — Engineered wood siding panels must bear on framing by at least 3/4 inch at any end joint to provide adequate fastening surface and prevent edge splitting. Insufficient bearing causes the panel end to deflect and crack under normal loading.

17. **C** — The proper sequence outward from the framing is: housewrap (WRB) → step flashing (over the WRB so it is part of the drainage plane) → siding (outermost). This ensures any water that penetrates the siding is captured by the step flashing and drains over the WRB, not behind it.

18. **C** — Uncoated (bright) steel and standard electrogalvanized nails corrode rapidly in the alkaline environment of fiber cement and cause rust streaking. Fiber cement manufacturers specifically require hot-dipped galvanized or stainless-steel fasteners to maintain warranty coverage.

19. **D** — Below 40°F, vinyl becomes more brittle and the material contracts, causing tighter fit at all joints and locks. Installers must allow extra clearance at panel laps and butt joints so the siding has room to expand when temperatures rise, and handle panels carefully to avoid cracking.

20. **B** — Housewrap repairs must use an approved patch of the same or compatible WRB material, oversized by at least 6 inches on all sides, lapped upper over lower per normal installation, and sealed at all edges with manufacturer-approved flashing tape. Duct tape and caulk are not durable WRB repairs.
