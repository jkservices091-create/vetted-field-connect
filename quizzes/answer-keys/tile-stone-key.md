# Tile & Stone — Answer Key

1. **B** — OSHA's respirable crystalline silica standard (29 CFR 1926.1153) requires wet methods as the primary engineering control when cutting silica-containing materials such as ceramic, porcelain, and stone tile. A wet saw or wet grinder dramatically reduces airborne dust at the source; an N95 respirator is required as a secondary control. Dry-cutting outdoors still generates dangerous concentrations.

2. **C** — Cement backerboard (e.g., HardieBacker, PermaBase) and foam tile backer systems (e.g., Schluter Kerdi-Board) are the tile industry standard (TCNA) and code-accepted substrates in wet areas. Standard and moisture-resistant drywall are not approved for direct tile application inside showers per TCNA Handbook guidelines and the IRC.

3. **A** — Modified thinset contains latex or other polymer additives that increase the mortar's bond strength, flexibility, and resistance to water and shrinkage cracking. These properties make it suitable for a wide range of substrates and tile types. Unmodified thinset is straight Portland cement and sand without polymer.

4. **B** — Most uncoupling membrane manufacturers (including Schluter for DITRA) specifically require unmodified (polymer-free) thinset for bonding the membrane to the substrate. The polymer in modified thinset retards moisture escape, preventing full curing under the low-permeability membrane and risking bond failure. Always follow the specific manufacturer's instructions.

5. **D** — Per ANSI A108.02 Section 4.3.7, the maximum allowable lippage for tiles with a grout joint of ⅛ inch or less is ¹⁄₁₆ inch (plus the inherent warpage of the tile). Lippage greater than this creates a tripping hazard, looks unprofessional, and accelerates edge chipping under foot traffic.

6. **B** — Unsanded (non-sanded) grout is used for joints ⅛ inch and narrower. Sand particles in sanded grout are too coarse to pack properly into narrow joints and can scratch polished or soft-surface tiles. Sanded grout is required for joints wider than ⅛ inch because the sand reduces shrinkage in the wider mass.

7. **C** — A manufacturer-listed liquid-applied waterproof membrane (e.g., Laticrete Hydro Ban, RedGard, Schluter Kerdi liquid) applied at the correct wet/dry film thickness, with all joints and corners reinforced per instructions, meets tile industry waterproofing standards (TCNA W244, ANSI A118.10). Felt paper, latex paint, and silicone-caulked drywall are not code-accepted shower waterproofing systems.

8. **B** — Starting from the center of the room and working outward ensures that cut tiles at opposing walls are symmetrical and balanced, which is the professional standard for most tile layouts. Starting from a corner or wall often results in narrow slivers on the opposite wall and an unbalanced appearance.

9. **B** — Epoxy grout is chemically resistant, non-porous, and easy to sanitize — properties required in food-service and commercial kitchen environments where grout is exposed to oils, acids, and frequent harsh cleaning. It is more expensive and more difficult to install than cementitious grout and is not necessary for standard residential use.

10. **B** — Natural calcareous and metamorphic stones (marble, travertine, limestone) are porous and readily absorb water, oils, and staining agents. A penetrating impregnating sealer fills the pores and dramatically reduces absorption. Sealing does not affect thinset bond strength and is not required before grouting (sealing before grouting would actually prevent grout adhesion to the stone face — a common field mistake).

11. **B** — The standard minimum slope for a shower floor is ¼ inch per foot toward the drain, consistent with plumbing code and tile industry guidelines (TCNA). A lesser slope causes water to pool; a steeper slope is acceptable but can make large tile installation difficult and cause lippage.

12. **B** — Large-format tiles bridge over low spots in the mortar bed, creating hollow spots with no support. The TCNA and ANSI standards require 95% mortar coverage for wet areas and 80% for dry, which flat-back large tiles often cannot achieve with a single trowel pass. Back-buttering the tile and/or using large-format mortars (with reduced open time slump) addresses this.

13. **B** — Movement joints are required at all changes of plane (floor-to-wall), over structural joints, and at maximum intervals across large tile fields (TCNA EJ171). They must be filled with an elastomeric sealant — never grout — because grout is rigid and will crack and pop tiles when the assembly moves thermally or structurally. Caulk at interior floor-wall corners is a minimum standard in every tile installation.

14. **B** — ANSI A137.1 establishes a minimum wet DCOF (Dynamic Coefficient of Friction) of 0.42 for level interior floors. Values below 0.42 indicate a slip hazard under wet conditions. Tiles for ramp or slope applications require higher DCOF values; the 0.42 threshold applies to level commercial and residential floor areas.

15. **B** — Schluter Kerdi and similar sheet membranes require a minimum 2-inch overlap at all seams, bonded with thinset, and manufacturer-supplied pre-formed fabric corners bonded at all changes of plane. These corners prevent the membrane from bridging improperly at inside angles, which is the most common point of waterproofing failure in sheet-membrane showers.

16. **B** — A ¼-inch × ¼-inch square-notch trowel is standard for most 12×12 floor tiles with polymer-modified thinset. Larger notches (½ inch) are used for large-format tile; small V-notches are for mosaic and wall tile. The notch size controls mortar thickness and coverage — too little mortar causes hollow spots.

17. **B** — Back-buttering applies a thin coat of mortar directly to the back of the tile before setting it into the mortar bed. This fills in the recesses of textured tile backs and, combined with the bed mortar, achieves the required full-coverage bond. Hollow spots under tile are a primary cause of cracked tiles and bond failure over time.

18. **B** — A hot-mop shower pan involves applying two to three layers of hot asphalt-impregnated felt over metal lath, with each layer mopped with liquid asphalt. The result is a continuous, seamless waterproof membrane that directs any water that penetrates through the tile and mortar bed to the drain's weep holes. It is a code-accepted alternative to liner membranes for shower pan waterproofing.

19. **B** — Rectified tile is ground to precise, consistent dimensions after firing. Because dimensional variation is minimal, joints as narrow as 1/16 inch are achievable and commonly specified for large-format rectified porcelain. Wider joints (¼ inch or more) are required for non-rectified tile to accommodate the dimensional variation inherent in the firing process.

20. **C** — Highly porous substrates or tile and hot, dry, or windy conditions draw water from the grout too rapidly, preventing it from curing properly and causing shrinkage cracks. The correct response is to lightly dampen the tile surface and substrate before grouting (without leaving standing water), grout in smaller sections, and work during cooler parts of the day. Pre-wetting is standard practice in difficult conditions.
