# Handyman & Punch List — Answer Key

1. **B** — Stud finders provide an approximate location; verifying with a small finish nail at the target height confirms the stud edge before drilling the final hole. The 16-inch rule is a starting point only — layout varies near corners, windows, and doors.

2. **A** — A drip at the base of the toilet (especially after flushing) is the classic sign of a failed wax ring. A cracked supply line would drip at the wall connection, and a failed flapper causes running water into the bowl without leaking at the base.

3. **B** — Silicone caulk is waterproof, mold-resistant, and remains flexible in permanently wet areas like tub surrounds. Latex caulk is paintable and easier to tool for dry interior trim work, but it deteriorates quickly in sustained moisture exposure.

4. **C** — A proper drywall patch over a hole larger than about 2 inches needs a rigid backing for the compound to bond to. Wood backing pieces or metal bridge clips hold the cut patch flush with the wall surface for a flat, durable repair. Self-adhesive mesh alone lacks the structural support for holes of this size.

5. **B** — Hollow-wall anchors are the solution when the mounting point lands between studs. For loads that demand firm anchorage in drywall, toggle bolts (which spread load across the back of the drywall panel) or snap toggles are appropriate. Stud mounting is always preferred for heavy loads when layout permits.

6. **B** — A loose top or bottom hinge is the single most common cause of a door sticking at the latch corner. Gravity causes the door to sag, rotating it so the top latch corner drags on the jamb. Tightening — or replacing with longer screws into the stud — fixes it before any planing is necessary.

7. **B** — A periodic single chirp every 30–60 seconds is the universal low-battery signal for smoke alarms. A continuous or repeated alarm pattern indicates smoke detection. End-of-life signals (if present) differ by manufacturer, often a distinct chirp pattern combined with an LED sequence.

8. **B** — Smoke alarms may be ceiling-mounted or wall-mounted with the top of the unit between 4 and 12 inches below the ceiling. "Dead air space" in corners and tight wall-ceiling junctions can delay smoke from reaching the sensor, so these zones are avoided.

9. **B** — A loose or unsecured electrical box is a safety hazard — it can pull free from the ceiling under the weight of a fixture or fan, causing a fall and potential electrical fault. The correct fix is replacing it with a properly secured, listed box rated for the intended load.

10. **B** — Paint on walls ages, fades, and develops a patina over time. Fresh paint — even from the same original batch — will rarely match aged painted surfaces exactly in either color or sheen. Feathering the touch-up into the surrounding area or repainting the full wall section minimizes the mismatch.

11. **B** — Polyurethane caulk adheres tenaciously to wood, metal, and masonry, is paintable, and resists UV degradation and temperature swings, making it ideal for exterior trim joints. Silicone is preferred in wet interior areas; latex is suitable for low-movement dry interior applications.

12. **B** — Before replacing a GFCI, the technician should verify the circuit topology: a tripped GFCI upstream on the same circuit will kill power to all downstream outlets. After confirming no upstream GFCI is tripped and the breaker is on, the device can be tested and replaced if it still won't reset.

13. **B** — Inside corners between tiled walls are change-of-plane joints subject to differential movement. Filling them with rigid grout causes the grout to crack as the walls flex. A flexible silicone or sanded caulk in a matching color is the correct material for inside corners in tile work.

14. **B** — A worn, warped, or misaligned flapper is the most common cause of a running toilet. The flapper lifts during flushing and must reseal completely on the valve seat. Even a minor warp or mineral deposit on the seating surface allows a continuous trickle into the bowl.

15. **B** — Pressing the button while listening at the chime establishes whether the circuit is complete. If there is no sound, testing button continuity with a multimeter (and then testing the chime transformer output) follows a logical diagnostic path rather than random component replacement.

16. **A** — Load assessment requires knowing the wattage of the new appliance (amps = watts ÷ volts), the breaker rating, and a reasonable estimate of what else shares the circuit. A 15-amp circuit at 80% continuous load capacity allows 1,440 watts; a 20-amp allows 1,920 watts before nearing the safe threshold.

17. **B** — A 45-lb TV with mount generates significant leverage (torque) against the wall. Lag bolts or structural screws driven into at least two studs provide the pull-out and shear strength needed. Drywall anchors alone cannot reliably support dynamic and static loads of this magnitude over time.

18. **B** — Pulling the caulk gun toward you deposits a consistent bead in the joint. Tooling immediately with a wet finger or caulk tool while the caulk is still fresh forces it into the gap and creates a concave, smooth finish. Pushing creates a piled, uneven bead.

19. **B** — Knob-and-tube wiring is ungrounded, may be damaged or covered with insulation (a fire risk), and requires evaluation of the full circuit condition by a licensed electrician. A handyman connecting new devices to active K&T wiring without proper assessment creates safety and liability exposure.

20. **B** — Adding a new branch circuit requires work at the main panel (breaker installation, conductor sizing, load calculation), permit pulling, and inspection — all of which fall under licensed electrical contractor scope in virtually every jurisdiction. Referring this work to a licensed electrician is the correct and professional response.
