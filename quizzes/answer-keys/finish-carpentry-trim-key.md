# Finish Carpentry & Trim — Answer Key

1. **B** — Coping is the professional standard for inside corners. The first piece butts square into the corner; the second is profile-cut (coped) to overlay the face of the first. Coped joints open less than miters as wood moves with humidity and are far more forgiving on out-of-square corners.

2. **B** — A scarf joint placed over a stud allows both pieces to be face-nailed through the joint into solid backing, creating a mechanically sound splice. A scarf joint in open air relies only on adhesive and will open with seasonal movement.

3. **B** — A ¼-inch reveal is the industry standard for door casing setback from the jamb edge. It creates a clean shadow line, hides minor inconsistencies in the jamb edge, and gives the casing a deliberate, finished appearance rather than looking like it ran short.

4. **C** — A 15-gauge 2½-inch finish nail penetrates through ½-inch drywall and at least 1½ inches into the stud, providing solid mechanical fastening. 4d and 6d nails are too short to reach framing reliably; 16-gauge brads are too small to hold wide baseboard flat against the wall.

5. **C** — IRC Section R311.7.5.2 sets the minimum stair tread depth (run) at 10 inches measured nosing to nosing. Shallower treads create a fall hazard by not providing enough foot support for a natural gait.

6. **B** — A bevel gauge captures the actual angle of the corner; bisecting that angle gives the correct cut for each miter piece. Assuming 45° on an out-of-square corner always produces an open joint that must be caulked or forced — an unacceptable finish result.

7. **B** — The IRC (Section M1602.2) requires that each habitable room have a return-air path to the HVAC system. When interior doors are closed without adequate undercut or transfer grilles, pressure imbalances develop and reduce HVAC efficiency. The standard undercut is ¾–1 inch above the finished floor.

8. **B** — Back-cutting removes material from behind the scribed face of the cope so that only the very edge of the profile contacts the adjacent piece. This prevents a bulge or rock at the joint face and produces a tight, crisp profile line even on slightly irregular surfaces.

9. **B** — When a wall assembly (framing + drywall) is thicker than the pre-hung door's jamb depth, a jamb extension strip is added to bring the jamb face flush with the finished wall surface so casing can be applied flat. Without it, the casing would need to bridge a step between the jamb and the wall.

10. **B** — The scribe tool must be set to the full width of the widest gap so that when it traces the wall, the pencil line on the trim represents exactly how much material to remove. Setting it to half the gap would leave a remaining gap after cutting.

11. **B** — Miter saws produce airborne wood dust and high noise levels (typically 95–105 dB). Safety glasses protect against projectile chips; hearing protection is required by OSHA whenever noise exposure approaches or exceeds 85 dB over an 8-hour shift — easily reached with repeated saw cuts.

12. **B** — IRC Section R311.7.5.1 sets the maximum riser height at 7¾ inches. Taller risers increase the risk of tripping and falls on stairs. Uniformity of riser height is equally important — variation is limited to ⅜ inch within any one stair flight.

13. **B** — The window stool must project beyond the side casings and have "horns" (returns or ears) that contact the wall on each side. This anchors the stool, provides a landing surface for the side casings, and is the traditional and code-compliant method for interior window trim.

14. **B** — A 45° scarf joint oriented with the face piece lapping away from the primary view direction means any slight opening of the joint is less visible. The joint is glued and face-nailed through both layers into framing. A butt joint on crown will always open visibly with seasonal movement.

15. **B** — A 15-gauge finish nailer drives nails long enough (2–2½ inches) to reach through the molding and into wall and ceiling framing, providing structural holding power. A 23-gauge pin nailer lacks the holding power for crown; a framing nailer would split the molding.

16. **B** — IRC R311.7.5.1 limits the variation in riser heights within a flight to ⅜ inch. Inconsistent riser heights are a leading cause of stair falls because the human body automates stride height; an unexpected step throws off balance.

17. **B** — A level chalk line establishes the true horizontal reference. The rail is shimmed to that line and the back edge is scribed to follow the wall contour. Following the wall bow produces a visually wavy rail; furring strips are overkill for trim work.

18. **B** — A ⅛-inch reveal between the door stop and the face edge of the jamb (and thus the casing) is the customary allowance. It hides minor inconsistencies in the door stop position and gives the casing a deliberate, inset appearance.

19. **A** — A 23-gauge pin nailer leaves a hole so small it is nearly invisible in painted trim and requires no filling in most cases. Finish nails require filling and sanding; construction adhesive alone is insufficient to keep wide baseboard flat; screws are inappropriate for visible finish trim.

20. **B** — In existing homes, re-plumbing a jamb is often impractical or destructive. The professional approach is to scribe the reveal line to the actual jamb face (maintaining a consistent ¼-inch reveal from the jamb edge regardless of plumb), then calculate and bisect the actual miter angle at the head so the joint closes tight.
