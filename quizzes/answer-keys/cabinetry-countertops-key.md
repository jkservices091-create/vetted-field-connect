# Cabinetry & Countertops — Answer Key

1. **B** — Full-height upper cabinets can weigh 80–150 lbs. A two-person lift with proper body mechanics (bend at the knees, load close to the body) prevents back injuries. Solo carries and stacking loads multiply injury risk.

2. **B** — Installation always starts at the highest point on the floor. Every other cabinet in the run is shimmed up to that reference plane. Starting at a low point means subsequent cabinets will go uphill and cannot be shimmed down.

3. **C** — A laser level or a long straightedge (6 to 8 feet) spans the full cabinet run and reveals the true high point accurately. A 4-foot level is too short to capture gradual floor slopes; visual inspection is unreliable.

4. **B** — Walls are rarely perfectly plumb or flat. A scribe strip is intentionally oversized and then scribed (marked to the wall contour) and planed or cut to fit, filling the gap while keeping the cabinet box perfectly plumb and level.

5. **B** — Installing blocking or a horizontal ledger in the wall gives a solid wood substrate to fasten into anywhere along the run. Drywall alone cannot support cabinet loads, and adhesive is not a structural fastening method.

6. **C** — The industry standard for bottom-of-upper-cabinet height is 18 inches above the countertop surface (typically 36 inches AFF), placing the bottom at 54 inches AFF. Designers sometimes specify 60 inches for taller users or open-shelf aesthetics; the installer follows the project specification.

7. **C** — Frameless cabinet boxes are joined through their side panels using clamps and specialized cabinet (confirmat or barrel) screws placed near the front and back of the sides inside the box, creating a rigid assembly without visible fasteners on the face.

8. **B** — European concealed hinges have three adjustment axes. Vertical (up/down) position is controlled by loosening or turning the vertical adjustment screw on the mounting plate. This is the first and simplest adjustment before resorting to more invasive corrections.

9. **B** — A standard countertop overhangs the cabinet face by 1 to 1-1/2 inches (commonly 1-1/2 inches) on the front and any non-seating side. This protects the cabinet face frame from drips and provides a natural grip edge.

10. **B** — Seams in laminate countertops should be located over a structural support point (a cabinet divider or rail) and away from the sink, where water exposure accelerates delamination at the seam. High-traffic cutting and prep areas should also be avoided.

11. **B** — Cutting a laminate surface from above causes the jigsaw blade's upstroke to chip the laminate. Scoring the face side with a utility knife first, then cutting from below with a jigsaw, keeps the visible top surface clean and chip-free.

12. **B** — Stone countertops must be supported continuously; unsupported spans over sink openings concentrate stress. Silicone (flexible) at the sink rim allows the stone and sink to move independently with thermal changes, preventing stress cracking that rigid adhesives would cause.

13. **B** — Dishwasher manufacturers specify mounting brackets that fasten to the countertop underside or adjacent cabinet sides. This is the correct, code-compliant anti-tip method. Adhesive and strap methods do not provide the rated restraint.

14. **A** — A diagonal rock means one pair of diagonally opposite corners is higher than the other. Placing shims under the low corners until all four are stable (verified with a level) is the correct fix. The wall will not pull a rocking cabinet into plumb without creating stress in the box.

15. **B** — The joint between a countertop and a tiled backsplash is a "change of plane" joint where two dissimilar materials meet. Both expand, contract, and settle differently. A flexible silicone sealant absorbs this movement; grout is rigid and will crack within months.

16. **B** — A ledger board temporarily fastened at the planned bottom-edge height of the upper cabinets lets one worker set a cabinet on the ledger, confirm plumb, and drive fasteners without holding the cabinet in the air. It is removed after all cabinets are secured.

17. **A** — Filler strips bridge the gap between the last cabinet in a run and the adjacent wall, appliance, or another run of cabinets. They maintain consistent reveal spacing and allow the cabinet doors and drawers to operate without hitting the wall.

18. **B** — A hairline crack in stone can propagate under load, especially over a sink opening. The correct professional action is to document the defect, inform the homeowner, and get a decision before installation. Installing a cracked slab without disclosure creates liability.

19. **B** — A 2-1/2- to 3-inch coarse-thread screw (often a #10 or #12 cabinet screw with a washer head) driven through the cabinet hanging rail and through 1/2-inch drywall into a stud provides the pull-out strength needed to support a loaded cabinet. Short drywall screws lack the thread engagement in the stud.

20. **B** — Dimension discrepancies affect appliance fit, countertop fabrication, and trim. Proceeding without documentation creates cost and accountability problems. The installer's job is to flag the issue and await direction — not to improvise or force the installation.
