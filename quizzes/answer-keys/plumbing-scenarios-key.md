# Plumbing — Scenario Quiz Answer Key

1. **B** — Polybutylene (PB) pipe was the subject of a major national class-action settlement (Cox v. Shell Oil, 1995) due to chlorine-related internal micro-fracturing that often shows no external symptoms until catastrophic failure. Simply replacing the water heater connections (Answer C) gives false reassurance — the risk is throughout the entire system. Completing the swap without disclosure (Answer A) exposes you to liability when (not if) the pipe fails. Refusing all work until a re-pipe is complete today (Answer D) is disproportionate — a plumber's duty is to inform and advise, not to hold work hostage.

2. **C** — S-traps are prohibited by the IPC (Section 906) because the trap can self-siphon, allowing sewer gas into the living space. They are not code-compliant in any occupied location, including basements. An AAV placed directly above an S-trap outlet (Answer B) does not fix the S-trap geometry — the vent must connect after a proper P-trap weir. Deferring the lav drain to trim-out (Answer D) leaves a known code violation in rough-in that will fail inspection. The correct fix while walls are open is to install a proper P-trap with an approved vent connection per IPC 901 and 903.

3. **B** — A burst pipe repair is urgent, but rushing without inspection risks missing additional freeze damage that will fail after you leave — and a callback with water damage is far more costly than taking an extra 15 minutes. Soldering immediately (Answer A) may be acceptable mechanically but omits inspection of adjacent piping and misses the opportunity to advise on freeze prevention. Replacing the entire branch (Answer D) without evidence of broader damage is scope creep that costs Mr. Vasquez significantly more than necessary. A documented recommendation to insulate or heat-trace the exterior wall cavity addresses the root cause.

4. **B** — Missing dielectric unions cause galvanic corrosion at the copper-to-steel joint, which can cause premature failure at the water heater inlet nipple — a known failure point. Identifying and documenting observed deficiencies outside your contracted scope is standard professional practice. It protects both the homeowner (who can make an informed decision) and you (documented advisory vs. silent observation). Installing the union without charging (Answer C) devalues trade work and sets a precedent. Declaring the heater must be replaced immediately (Answer D) is an overstatement — the dielectric union is a low-cost correction, not a replacement trigger.

5. **B** — A dry floor drain is a fast explanation for sewer gas, but a persistent gas smell near a furnace after the drain is refilled requires a separate investigation. Natural gas has mercaptan added specifically so it can be detected by smell. Treating the furnace smell as seasonal (Answer A) or as part of the drain issue (Answer C) misses a potentially life-threatening condition. If natural gas is detected near the furnace, the immediate protocol is gas shutoff and evacuation — not continued work. CO is also a risk with a malfunctioning furnace heat exchanger, making continued investigation essential before leaving the home.

6. **B** — Performing plumbing work without a required permit is a violation that can result in license suspension, fines, and homeowner title problems (unpermitted work can be flagged in real estate transactions and insurance claims). A GC's verbal request does not override your licensing obligations. Pulling the permit yourself (Answer C) without the GC's knowledge can create friction and may not be proper depending on permit ownership. Leaving work visibly exposed as a substitute for inspection (Answer D) is not equivalent to a permitted inspection and provides no legal protection. The permit process protects the homeowner, not just the contractor.

7. **C** — A gas smell inside a wall cavity near a gas appliance supply line is a potential emergency. The lower explosive limit (LEL) for natural gas is approximately 5% by volume — concentrations between 5% and 15% are explosive. Even a reading below LEL (Answer D) does not make the situation safe to continue work in; the source must be found and corrected before any work proceeds. Continuing (Answer A) or assuming it is an anode rod smell (Answer B) when the odor is coming from a supply penetration area — not from the water heater — risks ignition from any spark, including tools or light switches. The life-safety protocol is clear: evacuate and call 911 and the gas utility.

8. **C** — An improper toilet vent configuration will fail a rough-in or final plumbing inspection, and the IPC does not allow a toilet (a sanitary fixture with significant waste volume) to use the configuration described. An improperly placed AAV (Answer D) does not fix the fundamental venting geometry and may itself be in a non-approved location. Concealing the violation (Answer A) risks failed inspection and legal liability. The plumber's own crew created the error; transparent disclosure and proper correction — even at a financial loss — is both ethically and legally the right call. IPC 904 governs AAV installation requirements including accessibility.

9. **B** — A dishwasher drain connected without a high loop or air gap creates a cross-connection: if the sink drain backs up, contaminated wastewater can siphon into the dishwasher and potentially into the potable water supply. IPC Section 802.1 and manufacturer installation instructions require either a high loop or an air gap device. A check valve (Answer C) is not an approved substitute for an air gap in most jurisdictions. Accepting the previous improper installation (Answer A) makes you the installer of record on a code violation. Requiring a complete under-sink replumb as a condition (Answer D) is disproportionate — a high loop is a simple, inexpensive correction.

10. **B** — Atmospheric-vent water heater backdrafting beyond the cold-flue transient period is a CO hazard — combustion gases are entering the living space. Leaving a backdrafting appliance operational (Answers A, C, D) is not acceptable. Sealed utility closet doors are a common cause of combustion air starvation, which worsens with newer, tighter home envelopes — opening the door for testing is a key diagnostic step. If the cause cannot be corrected on this visit (blocked flue, disconnected flue section, inadequate combustion air), the appliance must be tagged out of service and the homeowner advised to install CO alarms immediately. IMC Section 701 governs combustion air requirements; ANSI Z21.10.1 covers water heater venting safety requirements.
