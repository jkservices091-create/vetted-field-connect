# Landscaping & Hardscaping — Answer Key

1. **B** — Calling 811 and waiting for utility marking is a legal and safety requirement before any excavation. Utility strikes can be fatal and create enormous liability. Survey plats and manual probing are not substitutes for an official locate.

2. **C** — Plate compactors generate significant noise (often 95+ dB) and whole-body and hand-arm vibration. Hearing protection prevents occupational hearing loss, and anti-vibration gloves reduce the risk of hand-arm vibration syndrome (HAVS) during extended operation.

3. **B** — Burying the base course provides lateral confinement and a level, compacted starting point. Setting on undisturbed soil or sod creates an unstable foundation that causes settlement, heaving, and wall failure over time.

4. **B** — Industry standards (ICPI) call for a minimum 4-inch compacted aggregate base for pedestrian paver areas and 6 inches for vehicular loads on stable, well-draining soils. Poorly draining or clay soils require additional depth.

5. **B** — Most segmental retaining wall manufacturers specify geogrid reinforcement when wall height exceeds their published unreinforced limit, typically 3–4 feet depending on the block system. The manufacturer's engineering data governs the specific threshold.

6. **B** — Water-saturated soil exerts hydrostatic pressure far greater than dry soil pressure. Drainage aggregate backfill and weep holes or drain pipe allow water to escape, relieving that pressure and preventing wall blowout or overturning.

7. **B** — A setback (batter) of approximately 1 inch per course leaning into the retained slope shifts the wall's center of gravity rearward, improving overturning resistance. It is a structural feature, not decorative, and is required at all practical wall heights.

8. **B** — The IRC and most local codes require a minimum 6-inch drop over the first 10 feet away from a foundation to direct surface water away from the building. This is one of the most common sources of basement water intrusion when it is absent.

9. **B** — Irrigation systems are a cross-connection risk because sprinkler heads can be submerged in standing water containing fertilizers, herbicides, or animal waste. A backflow preventer (pressure vacuum breaker or reduced-pressure zone device) protects the potable supply from contamination by siphonage.

10. **B** — Sod roots must grow into the underlying soil quickly. Tilling and amending the top 4–6 inches creates the loose, nutrient-rich zone the roots need. Smooth raking is secondary; without a prepared root zone, sod fails to establish even with perfect surface preparation.

11. **B** — A 2–4 inch mulch layer suppresses weeds and retains moisture without suffocating the root flare. Volcano mulching (piling against the trunk) traps moisture against the bark, promoting crown rot, disease, and pest infestation.

12. **C** — Tree stakes are a temporary stabilization measure for the root ball, not a permanent trunk support. Stakes left in place beyond one growing season prevent the trunk from developing taper strength and can girdle the tree with tie materials.

13. **C** — Planting at half the mature spread distance prevents mature branches from rubbing the building, roots from undermining the foundation, and keeps the plant from being deformed by the structure. The exact distance varies by species; this is the practical rule of thumb.

14. **B** — Cutting concrete and stone pavers generates crystalline silica dust, a known carcinogen that causes silicosis with repeated exposure. An N95 respirator is the minimum protection; wet-cutting suppresses dust at the source and is the preferred method wherever feasible.

15. **B** — A French drain intercepts and redirects subsurface water that would otherwise saturate soil, heave hardscape, or enter a basement. It is distinct from a surface swale (which handles sheet flow) and is not connected to the sanitary sewer.

16. **B** — USDA hardiness zones are based on the average annual minimum winter temperature for a region. They indicate whether a perennial plant can survive the coldest temperatures it will encounter, which is the primary factor in plant selection for a given location.

17. **B** — A standard plate compactor achieves effective compaction to approximately 4 inches of depth per pass. Lifts exceeding 4 inches leave uncompacted material beneath the surface, leading to settlement after installation.

18. **B** — Bedding sand for concrete pavers is screeded to 1 inch (approximately 25 mm) depth. Thinner beds don't allow for leveling variation; thicker beds compress unevenly under load and cause surface undulation over time.

19. **B** — After sweeping joint sand, the pavers must be compacted again with a plate compactor (using a protective rubber or foam pad to avoid surface damage) to vibrate sand into the joints, followed by re-sweeping until joints are consistently full. This locks the pavers together and prevents joint washout.

20. **C** — Large tree removal near structures carries serious risks of structural damage and personal injury. This work is outside the standard scope of landscaping and requires the training, equipment, and insurance of a certified arborist or professional tree service. Proceeding without that expertise creates unacceptable liability.
