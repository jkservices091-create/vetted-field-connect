# Site Prep & Excavation — Answer Key

1. **B** — Federal law (OSHA 29 CFR 1926.651) and the 811 "Call Before You Dig" system require utility locates before any excavation begins. Striking an underground utility can cause electrocution, gas explosions, or service outages.

2. **B** — Per OSHA excavation standards, Type B soil requires sloping at a maximum 1:1 (45-degree) angle. Type C requires 1½:1, and Type A allows ¾:1, so the ¾:1 option is too steep for Type B.

3. **C** — OSHA 29 CFR 1926.651(k) requires a competent person to inspect excavations daily before work begins and after any rainfall, freeze-thaw cycle, or other event that could change conditions.

4. **C** — Type A is the most stable classified soil, with an unconfined compressive strength of 1.5 tsf or greater, allowing the steepest permissible slope (¾:1). Sandy/granular soils are typically Type C, the least stable.

5. **B** — OSHA requires spoils, equipment, and materials to be kept at least 2 feet from the edge of a trench to prevent cave-ins caused by surcharge loads destabilizing the trench wall.

6. **B** — Water intrusion dramatically increases cave-in risk. Workers must exit immediately and the competent person must re-evaluate conditions before re-entry is permitted.

7. **B** — A benchmark is a known, fixed-elevation reference point (often a spike, nail, or monument) used throughout the project to control cut and fill elevations relative to a datum.

8. **C** — A laser level or optical level provides a consistent line-of-sight elevation across the site, allowing operators and finish-grade crews to match cut/fill elevations to plan requirements.

9. **B** — Silt fence must be trenched in and backfilled at the base (typically 6 inches buried) to prevent water from undercutting the fabric and bypassing the sediment barrier.

10. **B** — IRC and most drainage standards require a minimum of 6 inches of drop over the first 10 feet away from the foundation to direct surface water away from the structure and prevent moisture intrusion.

11. **B** — Establishing a clearly marked and enforced swing radius exclusion zone prevents workers from being struck by the rotating superstructure of the excavator, which is one of the leading causes of equipment-related fatalities.

12. **B** — Type C is the least stable classification and includes previously disturbed soil, granular or fissured soil, and any soil with water seeping in. It requires the flattest slope (1½:1) or full shoring.

13. **B** — A trench box protects only the area between its end panels. Workers must stay within that protected zone because the trench walls beyond the shield ends are unsupported and subject to collapse.

14. **B** — A builder's level or total station provides accurate optical elevation measurements across a site. Hand levels, line levels, and water levels lack the precision required for establishing a building pad elevation.

15. **B** — Dewatering lowers the groundwater table below the excavation level, preventing water accumulation that softens the subgrade, destabilizes trench walls, and creates hazardous working conditions.

16. **A** — Traveling straight up or down a slope keeps the machine balanced and prevents lateral rollover. Traversing across a slope shifts the center of gravity and significantly increases the risk of tipover.

17. **C** — Only the designated competent person has the authority and responsibility to inspect and clear a trench for re-entry after a rain event, per OSHA 29 CFR 1926.651(k).

18. **B** — Wattles are installed on the contour (perpendicular to the direction of flow) to slow sheet flow, promote infiltration, and capture sediment on slopes before it reaches storm drains or waterways.

19. **B** — 104.50 − 102.80 = 1.70 feet of fill needed. This is a straightforward elevation calculation that field workers must perform routinely when setting grade stakes or operating equipment.

20. **B** — Excavating below the 45-degree bearing plane (line drawn from the bottom of the adjacent footing at 45 degrees) undermines the soil that carries the footing load, risking settlement or collapse of the existing structure. Shoring or underpinning is required if excavation must go deeper.
