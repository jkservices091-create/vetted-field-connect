# Plumbing — Answer Key

1. **B** — The IPC requires a minimum 1/4-inch-per-foot slope for drain pipes 3 inches and smaller to maintain self-scouring velocity. Flatter slopes allow solids to settle; steeper slopes cause liquids to race ahead of solids.

2. **B** — Locating and closing the correct isolation valve and relieving residual pressure is the fundamental first step before any supply line work, protecting the worker and property from uncontrolled water release.

3. **C** — Water hammer arrestors are installed at the end of branch lines serving quick-closing solenoid or lever-handle valves (dishwashers, washing machines) to absorb the pressure spike when flow stops suddenly.

4. **C** — Copper is an electrochemically dissimilar metal to galvanized (zinc-coated) steel; direct contact in the presence of water causes galvanic corrosion. A dielectric union isolates the metals electrically.

5. **B** — The discharge must terminate in a location that will not cause burns, slipping, or property damage. Pointing it at a person's likely standing position or capping it are both prohibited.

6. **B** — The IPC limits the trap arm for a 1-1/2-inch fixture drain to 42 inches (3.5 feet). Longer runs allow the water seal to be siphoned out before the trap refills.

7. **B** — A 10-foot water column (approximately 4.3 PSI) held for 15 minutes is a common IPC rough-in water test requirement. The inspector observes the water level for any measurable drop.

8. **B** — Open flame inside a wall cavity is a leading cause of structure fires during plumbing work. Fire-resistant gloves protect hands and a heat shield (fire cloth or metal shield) protects framing behind the pipe.

9. **B** — A wet vent carries both wastewater and venting air in the same pipe, eliminating a separate dry vent run. It is commonly used for bathroom groups where fixtures are closely spaced.

10. **C** — PEX degrades rapidly when exposed to prolonged UV/sunlight. It must be protected or replaced with a UV-resistant pipe where it runs outside or through skylit spaces.

11. **B** — The stack base cleanout provides a rodding access point at the lowest point of the vertical stack, allowing drain-cleaning equipment to clear blockages in either direction along the building drain.

12. **B** — OSHA 29 CFR 1926.652 requires a protective system for any trench 5 feet or deeper. Between 5 and 20 feet, a competent person selects the appropriate sloping, shoring, or trench box.

13. **B** — A hose-bib vacuum breaker prevents back-siphonage of garden chemicals, standing hose water, or pool water into the potable supply when supply pressure temporarily drops.

14. **C** — Slow kitchen drains are most commonly caused by grease accumulation in the P-trap. Removing and inspecting it is quick, non-destructive, and typically resolves the issue before advancing to power-rodding.

15. **B** — Residential gas piping is commonly tested at 3 PSI (or per local AHJ), which is well above operating pressure, allowing leaks to be detected with a gauge or soap solution.

16. **B** — A long-sweep quarter bend (long 1/4 bend) has a larger radius that allows solids to travel smoothly around the turn. Short-radius elbows are prohibited in DWV drain lines because they create blockages.

17. **C** — Push-fit (SharkBite-style) connectors and manufacturer-approved crimp or clamp adapters are the correct way to transition from PEX to copper at a stop valve. Cement-based CPVC fittings are not compatible with PEX.

18. **C** — The IPC requires vent pipes to extend at least 6 inches above a sloped roof surface, but where the roof is flat or the vent terminates near HVAC intakes, local amendments often require 12 inches. Many codes and common practice default to 12 inches for all roofs.

19. **B** — Pressure imbalance causes scalding or cold shock in showers. A pressure-balancing or thermostatic mixing valve is the code-required remedy in most jurisdictions and corrects the root problem rather than masking it.

20. **B** — AAVs use a spring-loaded or gravity seal that opens inward to admit air when negative pressure (drainage flow) creates a partial vacuum, then seals shut to prevent sewer gases from escaping into the building. They cannot replace the main vent-through-roof required to relieve positive stack pressure.
