# Windows & Doors — Answer Key

1. **B** — Equal diagonal measurements (corner to corner across the frame) confirm the unit is square and not racked. A racked window will have operating sashes that stick or bind and corners that are not 90 degrees, even if individual sides appear plumb or level.

2. **B** — Standard rough opening sizing for a window unit is unit width + 1-1/2 inches and unit height + 1-1/2 inches. This provides 3/4-inch shimming clearance on each side and at top and bottom for leveling and adjustment. For a 36 x 48 unit the RO is 37-1/2 x 49-1/2 inches.

3. **B** — A sill pan flashing (sloped pan or L-flashing at the rough sill) is the last line of defense: it collects any water that infiltrates past the window frame or flange and channels it outward and downward to the exterior drainage plane rather than into the wall framing.

4. **B** — The correct sequence is sill first, then jambs lapping over the sill tape at the corners, then the head tape lapping over the jamb tapes. This shingle-lap sequence means each piece directs water outward over the piece below it, maintaining a continuous drainage plane around the opening.

5. **B** — Sealant is applied at the jambs and head to prevent water infiltration, but the sill must be left open (unsealed) at the bottom so any water that does get behind the window can drain outward. Caulking the sill traps water and accelerates rot and mold.

6. **B** — Shims placed at hinge and latch locations on the jambs provide bearing where the door hardware puts stress on the frame. Sill shims at the corners support the unit's weight. This pattern ensures the frame is uniformly supported without point-loading at unsupported spans.

7. **C** — IRC Section R310.1 requires sleeping room egress windows to have a minimum net clear opening of 5.7 square feet (or 5.0 sq ft at grade floor), a minimum net clear opening height of 24 inches, a minimum net clear opening width of 20 inches, and a maximum sill height of 44 inches above the finished floor.

8. **B** — IRC R310.2.1 sets the maximum sill height at 44 inches above the finished floor for egress windows. This ensures occupants can reach the sill and climb out in an emergency without a ladder or step.

9. **B** — The threshold weatherstrip must form a complete seal when the door is closed and latched. Any visible light gap at the threshold indicates an air and water infiltration path. The threshold height is adjusted so the door bottom compresses the seal without dragging excessively.

10. **A** — The installer must shim under the low corner of the door unit to bring the sill to level before fastening. Setting a door on an out-of-level sill causes the door to rack in its frame, resulting in binding, uneven gaps, and poor weatherstrip performance that cannot be corrected after installation.

11. **B** — Standard expanding foam (high-expansion) generates significant pressure as it cures and can bow or warp a window or door frame. Minimal-expanding (door and window) foam or backer rod with caulk fills gaps without exerting pressure that distorts the frame.

12. **A** — Self-adhered flashing tapes require a clean, dry surface to achieve full adhesion. Dust, moisture, or frost on the substrate causes poor bond and eventual delamination. Some tapes have minimum temperature requirements as well; cold temperatures may require warming the tape or substrate.

13. **B** — The full correct sequence maintains the drainage plane at every step: housewrap goes up first, sill flap is cut and folded into the RO and taped to the rough sill, the sill pan is flashed, the window is set and fastened, jamb flashing tape is applied, head tape is applied, and finally the housewrap head flap is folded down over the head tape and secured — each layer shedding over the one below.

14. **B** — Back-priming (applying primer to the back face and all end cuts) seals the wood against moisture absorption from the framing, flashing, or trapped condensation. Unprimed back faces absorb moisture differentially, causing the trim to cup away from the wall and exposing the back face to rot.

15. **A** — Binding at the top latch-side corner almost always indicates the hinge-side jamb is not plumb (leaning into the opening at the top). This closes the top corner on the latch side. The fix is to adjust shims at the hinge-side jamb until both jambs are plumb.

16. **B** — AAMA/WDMA/CSA 101/I.S.2/A440 (the North American Fenestration Standard, NAFS) is the standard used to certify windows and doors for structural performance, water infiltration, and air leakage. Hurricane zones also require large-missile impact testing (ASTM E1886/E1996) for glazing.

17. **B** — Nails should be driven straight through the flange slots (not angled) and not fully driven until the unit has been confirmed level, plumb, and square. Fastening the unit tight before checking alignment pulls it into whatever position it happens to be in and makes corrections very difficult.

18. **B** — OSHA 29 CFR 1926.501(b)(13) requires fall protection for residential construction workers at 6 feet or more above a lower level, including workers on ladders, scaffolding, or elevated platforms installing windows from the exterior.

19. **B** — The correct solution is to reduce the rough opening to the specified dimension by sistering or shimming the trimmer stud before setting the window. Relying on oversized shims in a gap too wide for the flange to span properly creates a weak installation prone to racking and air/water leakage.

20. **B** — Properly adjusted weatherstripping provides slight closing resistance (the seal is being compressed) and blocks all light and air movement around the closed, latched door. No resistance means the seal is not engaged; visible gaps mean air and water will infiltrate.
