# HVAC — Scenario Quiz Answer Key

1. **B** — EPA Section 608 regulations require technicians to have the ability to recover refrigerant before opening any refrigerant circuit — "topping off" through a service port does not open the circuit in the same way, but the absence of recovery equipment is still a regulatory compliance gap for any system work. More critically, R-22 is a phased-out refrigerant with supply restrictions and high per-pound cost; a low charge indicates a leak, and adding refrigerant without finding the leak violates EPA 608's prohibition on knowingly venting refrigerant into the atmosphere through a leaking system. Rescheduling and providing what help you can (filter, airflow check) is the professional and legally compliant response. Having a homeowner sign a waiver (Answer D) does not override federal EPA regulations.

2. **B** — Category IV furnaces produce condensing, slightly acidic flue gases under positive pressure. Type B double-wall metal vent is rated for Category I (negative pressure, non-condensing) appliances only. Connecting a Category IV furnace to a Type B vent and masonry chimney will cause rapid deterioration of the flue liner, allow condensate to saturate the masonry, and create a CO backdraft risk — a potentially fatal installation. A stainless flexible liner (Answer C) is rated for Category II/IV appliances in some configurations but is not the manufacturer-specified solution for PVC-vented condensing furnaces; the manufacturer's installation instructions and local mechanical code govern. A temporary connection pending a proper vent (Answer D) is never acceptable for a life-safety system.

3. **B** — A cracked heat exchanger with measurable CO in the supply air stream is an immediately dangerous condition. Carbon monoxide is odorless and colorless; 18 ppm in the supply air will accumulate to dangerous levels in a home over hours of furnace operation (NIOSH IDLH is 1,200 ppm; chronic low-level exposure causes health effects well below that). Sealing with furnace cement (Answer A) is not an approved repair and cannot reliably seal a heat exchanger fracture under thermal cycling. OSHA's 50 ppm threshold (Answer C) applies to workplace exposure — a residential home with children, elderly, or sleeping occupants warrants a far more conservative standard. The appliance must not be left operational.

4. **B** — Refrigerant misidentification is a serious regulatory and equipment risk. Mixing refrigerants violates EPA 608 regulations and destroys recovery cylinders' ability to be reclaimed. R-410A systems operate at significantly higher pressures than R-22 systems; introducing R-410A into an R-22 system can exceed pressure ratings of the older components, causing catastrophic failure. Manifold pressure readings (Answer A) are not reliable for identification — pressures can overlap or be misleading on a leaking system. Recovering into a mixed-refrigerant cylinder (Answer C) violates EPA 608 reclaim requirements. A refrigerant identifier tool is the required standard for positive ID before any service work.

5. **B** — Adding supply air to a space without an adequate return path violates fundamental HVAC system balance principles. The resulting positive pressure on the second floor will force conditioned air out through every penetration (worsening energy efficiency), create door slam hazards, and draw unconditioned exterior air into the first floor. The existing air handler at rated capacity cannot handle additional load without blower and coil issues. A 6-inch transfer grille (Answer C) is far too restrictive to serve as a return path for a meaningful amount of conditioned air. Mini-splits added on top of an improperly designed ducted extension (Answer D) do not fix the return air deficit for the first-floor system. Manual D duct design principles govern proper return air sizing.

6. **C** — The air handler and the outdoor condensing unit may share the same circuit or they may have separate circuits — but both must be de-energized and locked out before working on either piece of equipment. OSHA 1910.147 (control of hazardous energy) requires lockout/tagout at every energy source that could re-energize the equipment being serviced. Assuming the air handler has an independent breaker (Answer A) is incorrect for many split systems where the indoor unit is wired through the outdoor unit or shares the same circuit. The "one-hand rule" (Answer D) is a field myth — it does not provide protection from 240-volt contact or stored energy in capacitors. Proper LOTO with verified zero energy via meter is the only compliant procedure.

7. **B** — Combustion air starvation is a well-documented cause of furnace short-cycling, incomplete combustion, and heat exchanger stress. IMC Section 701 requires adequate combustion air for fuel-burning appliances in confined spaces — typically two openings totaling specific free area based on BTU input, or a single opening to the outdoors. A tight closet with no openings is a textbook confined-space combustion air deficiency. The door-crack test is a recognized field diagnostic method. Replacing the flame sensor (Answer A) treats a symptom. Increasing manifold pressure (Answer C) is dangerous and incorrect. Sealing the closet further (Answer D) worsens the condition and risks CO production and heat exchanger failure.

8. **B** — Condensate draining onto a crawlspace vapor barrier creates a chronic moisture source: a 3-ton air conditioner at typical latent load can remove 10–20 pints of water per day. This promotes mold, rot, elevated indoor humidity, and eventual foundation moisture issues. Float switches (Answer C) are a safety device for drain pan overflow, not a substitute for proper drain routing — they shut the system down rather than drain water appropriately. Adding a dehumidifier (Answer D) treats the symptom with added energy cost while the root problem continues. The IMC and most local mechanical codes require condensate disposal to an approved drain point; draining onto a crawlspace vapor barrier does not meet that standard.

9. **B** — The W/G reversal is the primary fault causing lockout: the furnace receives a fan signal where it expects a heat call, causing the gas valve or ignition board to behave unexpectedly. The reversal must be corrected at both ends of the wiring run if the DIY work affected both terminations. C-wire power-stealing causes inconsistent control voltage and erratic behavior in systems where the control board does not support it — proper C-wire connection eliminates this risk. Replacing the control board before correcting the wiring (Answer C) wastes a part. Adjusting lockout timing (Answer D) masks the fault without addressing the wiring errors. Full functional verification through both heating and cooling cycles confirms the repair before leaving.

10. **B** — Permit requirements for equipment replacement vary significantly by jurisdiction — some require permits for all HVAC work, some exempt like-for-like replacements, and some vary by equipment type or BTU input. Relying on a different contractor's verbal statement (Answer A) does not establish your compliance, and if they were wrong, the liability is yours. The AHJ (authority having jurisdiction) is the definitive source of permit requirements; most now publish requirements online or respond to brief calls. Operating without a required permit can void manufacturer warranties, create homeowner title/insurance complications, and expose your license to disciplinary action. Photographs (Answer C) and homeowner statements (Answer D) are not substitutes for a legally required permit.
