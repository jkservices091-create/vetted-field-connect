# Roofing — Answer Key

1. **B** — OSHA 29 CFR 1926.501(b)(13) requires fall protection at 6 feet or more above a lower level in residential construction. This applies to all residential roofing work regardless of pitch.

2. **B** — IRC Section R905.2.7.1 requires ice barrier (ice and water shield) to extend from the eave to a point at least 24 inches inside the interior wall line. In cold climates this prevents meltwater backing up under shingles from reaching the sheathing.

3. **B** — ASTM D226 No. 15 or No. 30 felt, or an approved synthetic underlayment, serves as the secondary water-resistive barrier under asphalt shingles. Rosin paper and poly sheeting are not code-compliant roof underlayments. Housewrap is a wall product.

4. **B** — Standard three-tab asphalt shingles are 12 inches tall with a 5-inch exposure (reveal), leaving a 7-inch headlap. This provides the double-layer coverage required by most shingle manufacturers and the IRC.

5. **B** — Manufacturer installation instructions (which govern warranty and code compliance) specify nails must be placed in the printed nailing zone above the adhesive strip. Nailing below the strip can puncture the sealed bond; nailing through the tabs on the course below causes splitting and missed substrate.

6. **B** — Step flashing consists of individual L-shaped metal pieces woven into each course of shingles at a sloped roof-to-vertical-wall junction (dormer, chimney side, addition). Each piece steps up with each shingle course to ensure water sheds away from the wall.

7. **B** — A kickout flashing (diverter) is installed at the lowest piece of step flashing to redirect water that would otherwise run behind the siding and into the wall cavity. Its absence is one of the most common causes of hidden rot at wall-roof intersections.

8. **B** — Balanced ventilation requires low intake (soffit/eave vents) and high exhaust (ridge or near-ridge vents). This creates a convective stack effect that removes heat and moisture from the attic year-round. Mixing intake and exhaust types at the same level short-circuits airflow.

9. **B** — In a woven (laced) valley, shingles from alternating courses of each roof plane are carried across and over the valley, interlacing as courses proceed up both planes simultaneously. This creates a fully shingled valley without exposed metal.

10. **B** — OSHA and ladder manufacturers require the base of a straight or extension ladder to be set out 1 foot for every 4 feet of working height (a 75-degree angle). This 4:1 ratio provides stability without being so steep the ladder tips backward.

11. **A** — In a closed-cut valley, shingles from one plane run past the valley centerline. Shingles from the other plane are then cut 2 inches back from the centerline (and trimmed at a slight angle), leaving a clean, tight appearance with water channeled down the valley.

12. **A** — IRC Table R905.2.7 requires a minimum 2-inch head lap for felt underlayment on roof slopes of 4:12 or greater. Steeper pitches shed water faster and generally require less lap, while low slopes require greater lap or double-layer coverage.

13. **A** — Tear-off should always start at the ridge and proceed downslope. This keeps the lower courses protected longer and prevents debris from being kicked down onto workers below. Stripping from the eave up pushes material toward anyone on the roof.

14. **B** — Most asphalt shingle manufacturers specify corrosion-resistant roofing nails with a minimum 3/8-inch head and a minimum 1-1/4 inch shank length (or long enough to penetrate through the decking by at least 3/4 inch). Shorter nails or staples do not meet most manufacturer warranty requirements.

15. **C** — Soft or spongy decking indicates rot or delamination that will not hold fasteners reliably. All compromised sheathing must be cut out and replaced with new material before any new roofing is installed. Covering bad decking voids the shingle warranty and leaves a structural deficiency.

16. **C** — OSHA 29 CFR 1926.502 allows employers to use personal fall arrest systems, guardrail systems, or safety nets. A harness and lanyard is the accepted personal fall arrest method when other systems are not feasible on a given roof geometry.

17. **B** — NFVA is the actual unobstructed airflow area through the vent, which is always smaller than the vent's physical opening because screens, baffles, and louvers reduce airflow. Code minimums for ventilation area are based on NFVA, not gross opening size.

18. **B** — At the eave, drip edge is installed under the underlayment so wind-driven water on the underlayment sheds onto the drip edge and into the gutter. At the rake (gable edge), drip edge is installed over the underlayment so water running off the top surface is also directed to the drip edge.

19. **B** — On steep slopes (above 8:12), warning lines and safety monitors are not permitted; personal fall arrest systems anchored to rated roof anchors are the required and most practical protection method per OSHA 29 CFR 1926.502.

20. **B** — Two-piece chimney flashing separates movement: the base (apron) flashing is nailed to the roof deck and moves with the structure, while the counter flashing is embedded in mortar joints and laps down over the base flashing, allowing the chimney and roof to move independently without breaking the seal.
