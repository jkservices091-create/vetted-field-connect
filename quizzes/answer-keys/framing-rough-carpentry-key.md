# Framing & Rough Carpentry — Answer Key

1. **C** — In floor joists, crown faces up so the load pushes the bow flat over time. In wall studs, crown faces out (toward the exterior) so the stud bears on its flat face and the drywall plane stays consistent on the interior.

2. **B** — 16 inches on center is the standard spacing for bearing walls in residential platform framing. 24-inch spacing is permitted in some applications (advanced framing) but requires engineered confirmation of header and plate loads.

3. **B** — IRC Section R302.11 requires fire blocking at every floor level and at mid-height of any stud cavity exceeding 10 feet. Its purpose is to slow vertical flame travel through concealed wall cavities.

4. **C** — The header spans the opening and transfers loads from above to the trimmer (jack) studs on each side. Cripple studs fill the gap above the header to the top plate; king studs run full height beside the trimmers.

5. **A** — Trimmer (jack) studs are cut to the rough opening height and support the ends of the header, carrying the header load down to the bottom plate or floor framing. They are distinct from king studs, which run full height and provide lateral support.

6. **B** — When panels are applied horizontally, every panel edge (including the horizontal seams) must bear on solid framing or blocking. Unsupported edges can buckle and reduce the shear capacity of the wall.

7. **C** — Hurricane ties (such as Simpson H2.5 or equivalent) provide a positive mechanical connection that resists wind uplift pulling the rafter off the wall and racking forces that try to push the roof off the top plate.

8. **A** — Equal diagonal measurements confirm the wall is square (a true rectangle). This is the standard 3-4-5 or diagonal-check method used before bracing and standing a wall frame.

9. **D** — Shear wall nailing schedules are set by the structural engineer on the drawings and vary by shear demand, panel thickness, and stud spacing. A blanket answer is dangerous; workers must read the shear wall schedule on the plans.

10. **B** — The rim joist (band joist) caps the ends of the floor joists, keeping them plumb and evenly spaced, and provides a continuous nailing surface for the wall bottom plate and exterior sheathing.

11. **B** — Header size is governed by span (opening width) and tributary load (roof, floor, or wall above). A small window in a one-story gable-end wall needs a much smaller header than a wide garage door carrying two floors and a roof.

12. **A** — IRC Table R602.3(1) and standard practice call for 16d nails in two rows at 16 inches on center, staggered, when face-nailing built-up headers. This provides the required shear transfer between the plies.

13. **B** — Staggering panel end joints distributes stress and prevents a continuous seam across the floor. Orienting the long dimension perpendicular to the joists maximizes the panel's bending stiffness across the span.

14. **C** — A standard rough opening for a 3-0 door is the door width plus 2 inches (for two 3/4-inch jambs plus shim clearance on each side), making it 38 inches wide. Height is typically door height plus 2 to 2-1/2 inches.

15. **B** — IRC R602.3.2 requires the upper top plate lap splice to be offset from the lower plate splice by at least 48 inches (4 feet) and located at a wall intersection or corner to maintain the continuity and load path of the double plate.

16. **B** — Safety glasses or goggles protect against flying fasteners and wood chips when nailing overhead, and hearing protection is required due to the high impulse noise of pneumatic nailers. A hard hat is also good practice but is not the specific answer to overhead nail gun work.

17. **B** — OSHA 29 CFR 1926.501(b)(13) requires fall protection at 6 feet above a lower level in residential construction. This applies to open floor decks, wall tops, and similar elevated work surfaces.

18. **A** — A standard 2x4 corner assembly is 3-1/2 inches wide. Subtracting 3/4 inch (half a stud width) from 16 inches gives 15-1/4 inches for the first layout mark, so that every subsequent 16-inch mark lands on the center of a stud and 4-foot sheathing panels break on layout.

19. **B** — Solid blocking directly under a bearing wall transfers the concentrated wall load to the joists below and prevents the joists from rotating (rolling) under eccentric loading. It also contributes to diaphragm action but its primary purpose here is load transfer.

20. **B** — Even a non-load-bearing partition wall concentrates weight along a line. Without a joist or blocking below, the subfloor spans unsupported and can deflect or squeak. Placing the wall over a joist or adding blocking between joists provides the necessary bearing.
