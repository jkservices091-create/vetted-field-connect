# Painting — Answer Key

1. **C** — EPA's RRP rule (40 CFR Part 745) triggers lead-safe requirements when painted surfaces are disturbed beyond 6 square feet per room for interiors or 20 square feet for exteriors. Smaller disturbances may still require testing but not the full containment protocol.

2. **B** — Gloss surfaces must be scuffed or chemically deglossed to give the new coat mechanical or chemical tooth for adhesion. Applying latex directly over an intact glossy alkyd surface without preparation is a leading cause of peeling failures.

3. **C** — Tannin-rich woods like cedar and redwood bleed water-soluble extractives that discolor latex topcoats. Oil-based alkyd primer or shellac-based primer (e.g., BIN) chemically blocks these stains; latex primers allow bleed-through on the first coat.

4. **B** — Solvent-based paints release organic vapors that are not filtered by particulate respirators. An organic-vapor cartridge (OV) respirator removes volatile solvents from inhaled air and is required by OSHA when vapor concentrations exceed permissible exposure limits in enclosed spaces.

5. **D** — Semi-gloss has a hard, non-porous film that resists moisture, cleans easily, and inhibits mildew growth — all critical in bathrooms. Flat and matte finishes are porous and trap moisture; eggshell is a compromise that works in low-humidity conditions but is less durable in steam-heavy environments.

6. **B** — Standard latex paints are touch-dry quickly but require 1–2 hours between coats under typical conditions to allow sufficient film formation and water evaporation before the next coat is applied. Recoating too soon traps moisture and causes lifting or solvent blushing.

7. **B** — EPA RRP interior containment requires covering the floor and furniture within the work area with plastic sheeting sealed to the baseboards/walls to contain lead dust and chips. The other options describe requirements that either don't exist or apply to different scenarios.

8. **B** — Efflorescence is water-soluble salt deposits pushed out by moisture migration through masonry. Painting over them traps moisture and guarantees delamination. The deposits must be mechanically removed (wire brush) and the surface treated with a dilute acid wash, then dried fully before priming with an alkali-resistant primer.

9. **B** — Tailing (elongated ends on the spray fan) is caused by insufficient atomizing pressure or a worn/partially clogged tip. Increasing pressure or replacing the tip produces a clean elliptical fan. Over-thinning causes runs, not tailing; a small tip produces a narrow fan, not tails.

10. **B** — Lap marks form when a wet coat overlaps a partially dried coat, causing a visible buildup line. Maintaining a wet edge — working quickly and overlapping brush strokes into the still-wet rolled section — prevents the partially dried boundary that causes lapping.

11. **C** — Canvas drop cloths absorb paint drips (preventing spread underfoot), are heavy enough to stay in place, and provide traction. Plastic poly sheeting is slippery and allows paint puddles to spread; rosin and kraft paper can tear and slide.

12. **B** — OSHA and ladder safety standards prohibit leaning beyond the side rails and forbid standing on the top cap of a stepladder. The only safe solution is to descend and reposition the ladder so the work is within comfortable reach at all times.

13. **B** — Shellac-based primer (alcohol-carried shellac) is the strongest stain blocker available — it seals heavy smoke damage, water stains, marker, and bleed-through that oil-based primers cannot fully stop. It dries very fast but is not suitable as a general-purpose primer for new drywall or exterior use.

14. **B** — Flashing occurs when patches or repaired areas have different porosity from the surrounding wall, causing uneven sheen and hide — the repainted spot appears lighter or duller. The fix is a full prime coat over the entire surface (or at minimum all repairs) before topcoating to equalize porosity.

15. **B** — Mildew is a living organism; painting over it traps spores and allows regrowth through the new film. The correct sequence is: kill and remove with a bleach/detergent solution, dry thoroughly, spot-prime with a stain-blocking primer, then apply a mildew-resistant finish coat.

16. **B** — OSHA 1926.1053 requires that extension ladders extend at least 3 feet above the upper landing surface (or roofline when accessing a roof). This provides a secure handhold when stepping on and off. The 4:1 rule places the base 1 foot out for every 4 feet of height — approximately 75°, not 60°.

17. **B** — Moisture in wood converts to vapor as the wood dries; if paint is applied before the wood reaches an acceptable moisture content (typically ≤15%), vapor pressure builds beneath the film and causes blistering or peeling. Deep penetration does not occur because the surface pores are already occupied by moisture.

18. **B** — A hide-optimized or tinted primer equalizes the dark base color in one coat, allowing one or two finish coats to achieve full opacity. Multiple finish coats over a dark surface are costly and may still show through; sanding to bare wood is unnecessary and impractical.

19. **B** — OSHA's flammable-liquids and painting standards require ventilation that keeps airborne solvent concentrations below 10% of the Lower Explosive Limit (LEL) and eliminates ignition sources (open flames, switches, motors). Recirculating the HVAC concentrates vapors throughout the building, which is the opposite of safe practice.

20. **C** — Latex paint requires temperatures at or above 50°F (some formulations 35°F with additives) during application and for the entire initial cure period — typically 24–48 hours. Cold temperatures prevent proper film coalescence, leaving a chalky, weak paint film. Thinning with water does not lower the freeze point meaningfully; alkyds also have temperature minimums.
