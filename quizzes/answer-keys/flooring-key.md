# Flooring — Answer Key

1. **C** — Most solid hardwood manufacturers require a minimum of 3–5 days of acclimation in the actual installation space at normal living conditions (60–80°F, 30–50% RH), and the wood should be checked to ensure its moisture content is within 2–4% of the subfloor before installation begins. Kiln-drying sets the moisture content at the mill, but transit and storage conditions can alter it.

2. **B** — NWFA guidelines and most LVP manufacturer specs require subfloor flatness of ³⁄₁₆ inch in 10 feet (or ⅛ inch in 6 feet). High spots cause hollow-sounding sections and joint stress; low spots cause bounce and potential clicking in floating floors.

3. **B** — The calcium chloride test (ASTM F1869) measures the amount of moisture vapor emitting from the slab surface over 60–72 hours. Results are expressed as pounds of moisture per 1,000 sq ft per 24 hours. It measures surface emission rate, not internal RH — which is the distinction from the in-situ probe test.

4. **A** — A pneumatic flooring nailer drives cleats or staples at approximately 45° through the tongue. This angle draws the tongue into the groove of the adjacent board while fastening it to the subfloor, and the fastener head is hidden by the next course's groove.

5. **C** — Most hardwood and LVP manufacturers require a minimum ½-inch expansion gap at all walls and fixed vertical obstacles. This space accommodates seasonal expansion and prevents buckling when the floor grows with humidity increases. Baseboard or shoe molding covers the gap.

6. **B** — Wood dust (especially certain hardwoods) is classified by OSHA and NIOSH as a respiratory hazard; some species are known carcinogens. An N95 particulate respirator is the minimum required protection during sanding. For species known to cause sensitization (e.g., oak, walnut), an N95 or P100 is recommended.

7. **C** — The in-situ relative humidity probe test (ASTM F2170) measures moisture at a depth of 40% of the slab thickness, capturing moisture that is still migrating toward the surface. It is more accurate than the surface-emission calcium chloride test for thick slabs or slabs over ground, which can have high internal moisture even when the surface appears dry.

8. **B** — Radiant heat systems warm the slab, which warms the flooring from below. Most LVP and engineered hardwood manufacturers specify a maximum substrate surface temperature of 80–85°F to prevent adhesive failure, excessive expansion, or delamination of the wear layer. Exceeding this limit voids warranties.

9. **C** — The NWFA and most manufacturers require a minimum 6-inch end-joint offset between adjacent rows to prevent a visually distracting "stair step" or clustered joint pattern and to maintain structural integrity across the floor surface. Some manufacturers require even greater offsets.

10. **B** — A reducer transitions between two floor surfaces of different heights — one higher, one lower — at doorways. A T-molding transitions between two floors of equal height; an end cap terminates flooring at a vertical surface; a threshold transitions at an exterior door.

11. **B** — High spots must be mechanically removed (grinding concrete, planning plywood) to bring the subfloor within the manufacturer's flatness tolerance. Thicker underlayment pads compress but do not bridge hard high spots; installing over an out-of-tolerance subfloor voids most flooring warranties.

12. **B** — Flooring-specific urethane or moisture-cure adhesives are formulated to tolerate slab moisture and provide the elongation characteristics needed to accommodate flooring movement. The trowel notch size is critical: too little adhesive causes hollow spots; too much causes the floor to float and shift. General construction adhesives lack the flexibility and moisture resistance required.

13. **C** — Rosin paper and 15-lb felt act as a slip sheet that allows the hardwood to move slightly without friction against the subfloor, preventing squeaks caused by wood-on-wood rubbing. These materials are not vapor barriers and do not provide meaningful thermal insulation.

14. **B** — Squeaks in nail-down hardwood are almost always caused by the flooring or the subfloor moving slightly under load. Under-fastened flooring rubs on the subfloor; a subfloor not adequately screwed to the joists allows the subfloor itself to move. Increasing fastener density and screwing the subfloor to joists resolves most squeaks.

15. **B** — EPA's RRP rule applies to renovation work in pre-1978 housing. Sanding floors in such a building can disturb lead-containing paint on the floor surface or adjacent baseboards, requiring HEPA-equipped equipment and other lead-safe work practices to contain and properly dispose of the dust.

16. **B** — Laminate underlayment must be loose-laid in a single layer with no overlapping or doubled-up areas, which would create uneven support and cause joint stress. Seams should be offset and taped per manufacturer instructions. Underlayment stapled or glued to the subfloor prevents the floating floor from moving freely.

17. **B** — An "H" pattern occurs when end joints in every other row align vertically. This creates a structural weak line and is visually objectionable. Industry standards require random stagger specifically to avoid this pattern.

18. **A** — NWFA guidelines require a minimum ¾-inch APA-rated plywood or OSB subfloor for nail-down solid hardwood. The structural panel must be properly fastened to the joists with no deflection. Gypsum and fiber cement boards are too brittle; hardboard is too thin and not structural.

19. **B** — Engineered hardwood's cross-ply construction (perpendicular wood layers) dramatically reduces expansion and contraction across the plank width compared to solid hardwood, making it suitable for concrete slab installation and environments with greater humidity variation. Many engineered products can be lightly sanded and refinished 1–2 times.

20. **B** — Proper dust containment requires sealing off the work area with polyethylene sheeting, using HEPA-equipped (or equivalent) sanding machines, and creating negative air pressure so dust flows into the work area rather than out to adjacent spaces. Running the HVAC on high disperses fine dust throughout the building — a common and serious mistake.
