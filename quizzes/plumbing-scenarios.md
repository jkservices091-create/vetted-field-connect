# Plumbing — Scenario Quiz

**Questions:** 10  |  **Passing score:** 8/10 (80%)  |  **Time limit:** 25 minutes

These are real-world judgment scenarios. Each question describes a job-site situation; pick the SINGLE BEST course of action. There is often more than one acceptable answer in real life — choose the one that best balances safety, code, and customer outcome.

---

1. **Scenario:** You arrive at a 1980s ranch home to replace a water heater for Mrs. Hendricks. As you disconnect the cold supply line you notice the supply pipe is gray plastic — polybutylene (PB), identifiable by the gray color and "PB2110" stamp. The pipe appears intact at the connection point but you recall that PB is notorious for internal degradation and fitting failures. Mrs. Hendricks is unaware of this and just wants the new water heater installed today.

   **What do you do?**
   A. Complete the water heater swap using the existing PB connections — they look fine at this point, so there's no immediate problem to address.
   B. Stop all work, advise Mrs. Hendricks that polybutylene piping is present in the home, explain the history of class-action settlements and failure risk, document your findings in writing, and recommend a full-home PB re-pipe evaluation before completing the water heater installation.
   C. Replace only the short section of PB that connects to the new water heater with copper or PEX and say nothing further — you fixed the part you touched.
   D. Refuse to complete any work until Mrs. Hendricks agrees in writing to a full re-pipe today.

2. **Scenario:** You are roughing in a bathroom in a finished basement for Mr. Osei. The toilet, lav, and shower drain all tie into a horizontal run heading to a cleanout. While confirming the vent routing, you realize there is no vent connection at all — the previous rough-in left an S-trap configuration on the lav drain rather than a proper P-trap with a vent. The walls are still open and the inspection is scheduled for next week.

   **What do you do?**
   A. Leave the S-trap — it is code-compliant in basements because the fixture is below grade.
   B. Install an air admittance valve (AAV) directly above the S-trap outlet to provide the missing vent function without rerouting drain lines.
   C. Remove the S-trap and install a proper P-trap with a vent pipe connected to the building vent system (or a listed AAV in an accessible location above the trap weir per IPC), since S-traps are not permitted under the IPC due to self-siphoning risk.
   D. Cap the lav drain for now, complete the toilet and shower rough-in, and address the lav vent at trim-out.

3. **Scenario:** You are called to a 1970s colonial home on a cold January morning. The homeowner, Mr. Vasquez, says a supply pipe in the exterior wall of the kitchen burst overnight due to freezing. Water has been shut off at the main. When you open the wall, you find a burst 1/2-inch copper pipe and standing water in the wall cavity. Mr. Vasquez asks you to repair the pipe as fast as possible because his family has no water.

   **What do you do?**
   A. Solder a repair coupling immediately — the pipe has already thawed since the water is off and the home is warm.
   B. Dry the wall cavity, repair the burst section with a copper slip coupling or push-fit repair fitting, then inspect for additional freeze damage upstream and downstream before restoring water — restore service only after confirming no other failures and advising Mr. Vasquez to insulate or heat-trace the exterior wall cavity to prevent recurrence.
   C. Repair the pipe but leave the wall open and skip insulation advice — freeze prevention is outside your scope as a plumber.
   D. Replace the entire kitchen supply branch from the main riser to every fixture today since freeze damage may have weakened the whole run.

4. **Scenario:** While installing a new utility sink in a laundry room for Ms. Cho, you notice that the existing water heater nearby has no dielectric union on the cold inlet — a copper pipe connects directly to a galvanized steel nipple threading into the water heater. The homeowner did not hire you to work on the water heater. The water heater appears to be about 7 years old.

   **What do you do?**
   A. Ignore it — you were hired for the utility sink only, and pointing out extra work looks like upselling.
   B. Note the missing dielectric union in your written job record, inform Ms. Cho that the direct copper-to-galvanized connection accelerates corrosion at the inlet and can shorten water heater life, and offer to install the proper dielectric union as a separate line item — but proceed with your contracted scope regardless of her decision.
   C. Disconnect the water heater supply and install a dielectric union at no charge since it is a quick fix and prevents a future call.
   D. Tell Ms. Cho the water heater needs to be replaced immediately and that you cannot proceed with the sink until the heater is updated.

5. **Scenario:** You are called to investigate a sewer gas smell in the finished basement of the Nguyen family's 1990s two-story home. You trace the smell to the floor drain near the utility sink. When you pour water into the floor drain, the smell briefly gets worse before dissipating. The floor drain has clearly been dry for a long time. However, you also notice a slight sulfur smell persisting near the furnace even after the floor drain trap is refilled.

   **What do you do?**
   A. Refill the floor drain trap, declare the problem solved, and leave — the furnace smell is likely just the first run of the heating season.
   B. Refill the floor drain trap to address that source, but continue investigating the persistent furnace-area smell: check for a cracked furnace heat exchanger, inspect the condensate drain, and test for combustion gas leaks at the gas connections before leaving the home. If you smell natural gas at the furnace supply, shut off the gas and call the gas utility.
   C. Treat the whole basement with enzyme-based drain cleaner poured down every drain and advise the Nguyens to run all basement fixtures weekly.
   D. Tell the Nguyens the smell is harmless methane from the sewer and that floor-drain evaporation is normal.

6. **Scenario:** A general contractor, Tom Balley, asks you to install a new bathroom in a home addition without pulling a plumbing permit. He says "the inspector never comes out to this neighborhood" and that a permit "will just delay the job two weeks." The work involves new DWV rough-in, supply lines, and a new connection to the main sewer stack.

   **What do you do?**
   A. Agree to skip the permit since the GC is taking responsibility for the project and the work is standard.
   B. Explain to Tom that you are not able to perform new DWV and supply work without a permit — a permit protects the homeowner's title, ensures code compliance, and your license can be suspended or revoked for unpermitted work. Offer to handle the permit application yourself to minimize the delay.
   C. Pull the permit yourself without telling Tom and absorb the inspection scheduling cost in your bid.
   D. Proceed without a permit but leave exposed all rough-in work so it can be inspected informally if anyone asks.

7. **Scenario:** You are replacing a shower valve in a second-floor bathroom of a 2003 home for Dr. and Dr. Park. As you cut into the supply lines, you smell a strong sulfur/rotten egg odor coming from the wall cavity near the water supply penetration through the floor plate. It is not a drain smell — it is distinctly gas-like. The gas furnace and gas range are both on the first floor directly below.

   **What do you do?**
   A. Open a window, finish the supply line work quickly, and mention the smell to the Parks when you're done.
   B. Continue the valve replacement — the smell is most likely sulfur bacteria in the hot water heater anode rod, which is common.
   C. Stop work immediately. Evacuate yourself and the homeowners, do not operate any switches or create sparks, leave doors open as you exit, call 911 and the gas utility from outside the structure. Do not re-enter until the gas utility clears the building.
   D. Use a gas detector from your truck to check the wall cavity; if the reading is below the lower explosive limit, proceed with the job and note the reading in your paperwork.

8. **Scenario:** You are completing a master bathroom remodel for Mr. and Mrs. Abernathy. During trim-out, you discover the vent pipe for the new toilet was tied into an existing vent stack that serves the kitchen drain — and your helper connected it below the kitchen fixture's trap arm rather than above it, making it a wet vent in a configuration not permitted by the IPC for toilets. The tile work is done and opening the wall to reroute will cost approximately $1,200 in tile repair — significantly more than your original rough-in allowance.

   **What do you do?**
   A. Leave it and hope the inspector does not notice — the toilet flushes fine in testing.
   B. Install a large-capacity AAV above the toilet's trap weir to compensate for the improper vent connection and avoid opening the wall.
   C. Disclose the improper vent configuration to the Abernathys, explain that the IPC does not permit the current arrangement, provide a written estimate for the correction including tile repair, and correct it — absorbing some or all of the overrun since the error was your crew's. Attempting to conceal a code violation or patch it with an improper AAV installation exposes the homeowner to failed inspection and you to license liability.
   D. Correct the vent by cutting the vent pipe at the stack and capping it, then using a studor/AAV at the toilet — without inspecting whether the AAV is accessible or in an approved location.

9. **Scenario:** You arrive to install a new dishwasher for a homeowner and find the existing dishwasher drain hose is connected directly to the drain tailpiece of the kitchen sink — no high loop and no air gap. The homeowner, Ms. Fontaine, says it has "always been that way" and that the previous plumber installed it. She just wants the new dishwasher swapped in the same way.

   **What do you do?**
   A. Connect the new dishwasher drain the same way — if it passed before, it will pass again.
   B. Connect the new dishwasher drain with a proper high loop secured to the underside of the countertop or an air gap device at the sink rim per IPC cross-connection control requirements, explain the backflow risk to Ms. Fontaine, and note the correction in your invoice.
   C. Install a check valve on the dishwasher drain in addition to the direct connection — this is equivalent to an air gap.
   D. Refuse to complete the installation unless Ms. Fontaine pays for a full under-sink replumb to add an air gap device, since the existing drain layout is nonstandard.

10. **Scenario:** You are called to a 1950s Cape Cod home to investigate a water heater that is backdrafting. The homeowner, Mr. Jablonski, says the unit was recently serviced by another company. You hold a smoke pencil at the draft hood and the smoke is clearly pulled into the unit rather than up the flue during a 5-minute observation period after a cold start. The water heater is in a utility closet with the door closed.

    **What do you do?**
    A. The backdraft is temporary (cold flue effect) — advise Mr. Jablonski to leave the closet door open during initial startup and the problem will resolve itself.
    B. Confirm the backdraft is not just a cold-flue transient by testing at full operating temperature. If backdrafting persists: check for blockages in the flue, verify combustion air is adequate in the closet (the sealed door may be starving the burner), inspect the flue pipe for disconnection or deterioration, and do not leave the appliance operating in a backdrafting condition — carbon monoxide is entering living space. Tag the unit out of service if the cause cannot be corrected on this visit and advise Mr. Jablonski to get CO alarms installed immediately.
    C. Replace the draft hood — it is probably deformed from the previous service visit.
    D. Advise Mr. Jablonski to upgrade to a power-vent or direct-vent water heater on a future visit; backdrafting is a known issue with atmospheric-vent units in tight houses.
