# HVAC — Scenario Quiz

**Questions:** 10  |  **Passing score:** 8/10 (80%)  |  **Time limit:** 25 minutes

These are real-world judgment scenarios. Each question describes a job-site situation; pick the SINGLE BEST course of action. There is often more than one acceptable answer in real life — choose the one that best balances safety, code, and customer outcome.

---

1. **Scenario:** You arrive at the Mendoza family's 1990s two-story home for a routine A/C maintenance call on a hot July afternoon. The system is low on charge and you are ready to add refrigerant. When you open your truck, you realize you left your refrigerant recovery machine at the shop. The system uses R-22 and needs approximately 1 lb of additional charge. You have a small cylinder of R-22 on the truck. The homeowners are home and the indoor temperature is 84°F.

   **What do you do?**
   A. Add the pound of R-22 — the system is low and the refrigerant isn't being vented, just added.
   B. Explain to the Mendozas that you cannot legally add or service a refrigerant circuit without recovery equipment on-site (EPA 608 requires the ability to recover before opening a system), reschedule for when you have all required equipment, and provide a temporary fix such as verifying airflow and filter condition while you arrange to return.
   C. Add the refrigerant quickly since you are not opening the system in a way that releases refrigerant — topping off through the service port does not require recovery.
   D. Have the Mendozas sign a waiver acknowledging you did not have recovery equipment and proceed.

2. **Scenario:** You are installing a replacement gas furnace in a 1970s ranch home for Mr. Hoffman. The new furnace is a 96% AFUE two-stage unit — a Category IV appliance requiring a sealed, positive-pressure PVC vent. The existing setup uses the original 4-inch Type B double-wall metal vent pipe running up through the interior chase to the masonry chimney. Mr. Hoffman's brother-in-law told him "just use the old chimney" to save money.

   **What do you do?**
   A. Connect the new furnace exhaust to the existing Type B vent — the chimney has been working fine for 50 years.
   B. Explain to Mr. Hoffman that a Category IV furnace must use a sealed, corrosion-resistant vent system (typically PVC/CPVC) because its flue gases are condensing and under positive pressure — the old Type B vent and masonry chimney are not compatible with this appliance and would allow condensate to deteriorate the flue lining and create CO back-draft risk. Install the proper PVC vent per the manufacturer's instructions and local mechanical code.
   C. Use the existing chimney but line it with a stainless-steel flexible liner — it is a good compromise between cost and code.
   D. Connect the new furnace to the Type B vent temporarily so the homeowner has heat, then schedule a return visit for a proper PVC vent upgrade.

3. **Scenario:** You are performing a pre-season maintenance inspection on a gas furnace for Mrs. Albright. When you remove the access panel to inspect the heat exchanger, you see a visible crack in the primary heat exchanger — a roughly 1-inch fracture near the top of the second cell. You run a combustion gas test downstream of the heat exchanger in the supply air plenum and detect trace levels of CO (18 ppm). The system is otherwise functional. Mrs. Albright says she just wants you to "get it running" for the winter.

   **What do you do?**
   A. Seal the crack with high-temperature furnace cement and advise Mrs. Albright to have the heat exchanger replaced next season.
   B. Document the cracked heat exchanger and CO detection with photos and written findings, shut down the furnace and lock it out, advise Mrs. Albright clearly that operating a furnace with a cracked heat exchanger introduces CO into living space (a potentially fatal condition), and provide options: replace the heat exchanger if cost-effective or replace the furnace. Do not leave the furnace operational.
   C. Leave the furnace running since 18 ppm CO is below OSHA's 50 ppm action level and the crack is small.
   D. Advise Mrs. Albright to open a window in the mechanical room and run the furnace only on low-stage until she budgets for a replacement.

4. **Scenario:** You are performing a service call on a split-system air conditioner for Mr. and Mrs. Kim. You identify a refrigerant leak at the indoor coil. When you check the outdoor unit's service ports to verify refrigerant type before recovery, the label is sun-bleached and illegible. The line set connections and coil appear relatively new — possibly replaced within the last 5 years. You carry both R-410A and a small amount of R-22 recovery cylinders.

   **What do you do?**
   A. Connect your gauges and sample the manifold reading — if the pressures look like R-410A, charge it as R-410A.
   B. Before recovering or adding any refrigerant, positively identify the refrigerant type using a refrigerant identifier tool or by locating the original equipment model/serial number to look up the factory charge specification. Mixing refrigerants is a violation of EPA 608 regulations and can destroy the compressor. Do not proceed until the type is confirmed.
   C. Recover whatever is in the system into a generic mixed-refrigerant cylinder and recharge with R-410A since most systems of this age use R-410A.
   D. Ask the homeowners which refrigerant was used when the previous tech serviced it — their recollection is sufficient to proceed.

5. **Scenario:** You are called to add central A/C to the second floor of a 1985 colonial for the Nguyen family. The existing system serves only the first floor with a single air handler in the basement. The homeowners want you to tap the existing ductwork to extend to the second floor. When you assess the system, you find the existing air handler is already at its rated airflow capacity and there is no return air path from the second floor — the only return is a single large grille in the first-floor hallway.

   **What do you do?**
   A. Extend the supply ducts to the second floor as requested — adding more supply diffusers will push conditioned air upstairs.
   B. Explain to the Nguyens that adding second-floor supply without a return air path will pressurize the second floor and depressurize the first, causing comfort problems, door slam issues, increased infiltration, and system inefficiency — a proper addition requires either a dedicated second system, or a comprehensive duct redesign including a second-floor return, and possibly a larger air handler. Provide a scope that addresses the full system requirements rather than a partial fix.
   C. Add supply ducts to the second floor and cut a 6-inch transfer grille in the floor between levels — this solves the return air problem cheaply.
   D. Upsell the homeowners to a mini-split for the second floor and tap the existing system anyway for partial coverage.

6. **Scenario:** You arrive to perform a service call on an air handler in a utility closet. The service disconnect is located around the corner of the house at the outdoor condenser unit, approximately 40 feet away from where you need to work on the air handler. A handwritten note from a previous tech on the indoor unit says "disconnect is at outdoor unit." You are about to open the electrical compartment of the air handler to diagnose a control board issue.

   **What do you do?**
   A. The outdoor disconnect cuts power to the outdoor unit only — the air handler has its own breaker in the panel, so the outdoor disconnect is irrelevant here.
   B. Trip the main circuit breaker serving the air handler at the electrical panel, then open the air handler cabinet.
   C. De-energize the circuit at the electrical panel, walk to the outdoor disconnect and open it as well, apply lockout/tagout at both the panel breaker and the outdoor disconnect, verify zero voltage at the air handler terminals with a meter, and only then open the electrical compartment.
   D. Open the electrical compartment carefully and use one hand only — a "one-hand rule" is sufficient protection if you stay away from ground.

7. **Scenario:** While installing a new 96% AFUE gas furnace in a tight, recently weatherized home for Ms. Patterson, you notice the mechanical room (a small utility closet, approximately 40 sq ft) has no dedicated combustion air openings. The previous 80% furnace had been installed without combustion air openings as well. The new furnace is a non-direct-vent (two-pipe) model selected by the homeowner's HVAC designer. After startup, the furnace flame sensor trips on low flame shortly after ignition.

   **What do you do?**
   A. Replace the flame sensor — low-flame nuisance trips on a new furnace are usually a defective sensor.
   B. Check for combustion air deficiency: test by cracking the utility closet door — if the furnace runs normally with the door open, the confined space lacks adequate combustion air. Install the required permanent combustion air openings per IMC Section 701 before completing the installation, and advise Ms. Patterson's designer that the two-pipe (non-direct-vent) furnace selection requires combustion air provisions the space does not currently have.
   C. Adjust the gas valve to increase manifold pressure — the flame is too weak because of insufficient gas input.
   D. Seal all gaps in the utility closet to prevent the furnace from drawing cold outside air that causes the low-flame condition.

8. **Scenario:** You are installing a new split-system A/C replacement for Mr. Torres. The existing condensate drain from the air handler in the crawlspace terminates by draining directly onto the crawlspace vapor barrier. The installer before you has been doing it this way for years, and there is no floor drain in the crawlspace. Mr. Torres says "every house in this neighborhood does it that way."

   **What do you do?**
   A. Connect the new unit's condensate drain the same way — it is standard practice locally.
   B. Route the condensate drain to daylight (outside the structure through the foundation wall) or to an approved drain point, and explain to Mr. Torres that draining onto a crawlspace vapor barrier promotes mold growth, wood rot, elevated humidity, and eventual foundation moisture intrusion — the cost to route it properly now is far less than remediation later. Document your corrective routing in the installation record.
   C. Install a float switch in the drain pan as a substitute for proper drain routing — if the pan fills, the system shuts off before overflow.
   D. Drain into the crawlspace but add a dehumidifier downstream to manage the added moisture.

9. **Scenario:** You arrive at the Garza family's home for a thermostat replacement. A DIY thermostat was recently installed by the homeowner's son. The system is not heating: the furnace runs for 15 seconds then locks out. As you trace the wiring, you find the W (heat) and G (fan) terminals on the air handler control board have been reversed. Additionally, the C-wire was not connected at the thermostat, so the new smart thermostat is power-stealing from the R wire through the heating circuit.

   **What do you do?**
   A. Correct the W/G reversal at the thermostat only and restart the system — the power-stealing behavior is normal for smart thermostats.
   B. Correct the W/G reversal at both the thermostat and the air handler control board (verify both ends match the wiring diagram), connect the C-wire properly at the thermostat, and verify correct system operation through a full heating and cooling cycle before leaving — power-stealing can cause control board damage and erratic behavior in systems not designed for it.
   C. Replace the control board since it likely was damaged by the reversed wiring, then correct the thermostat wiring.
   D. Rewire the thermostat to match what the DIY installer did and adjust the furnace lockout timing to allow a longer trial-for-ignition period.

10. **Scenario:** A homeowner, Mr. Beaumont, wants you to swap out his existing 3-ton split system with a new 3-ton system. When you ask about the permit, he says the previous company told him a like-for-like equipment swap "never needs a permit" in his jurisdiction. You are licensed and insured and this is the first time you've worked in this particular municipality. The job would be a straightforward equipment swap — same location, same line set, same duct connections.

    **What do you do?**
    A. Proceed without a permit — if the previous company said it is not required, that is their professional judgment and you can rely on it.
    B. Before starting work, contact the local AHJ or check their online portal to confirm permit requirements for a like-for-like system replacement in that municipality. Requirements vary by jurisdiction. If a permit is required, pull it before starting — your license and insurance coverage may be voided on unpermitted work, and installation without a required permit can create title and insurance issues for the homeowner.
    C. Proceed without a permit but keep detailed installation photos in case any question arises later.
    D. Ask Mr. Beaumont to sign a statement saying he was told no permit was needed, and use that as documentation if questions arise.
